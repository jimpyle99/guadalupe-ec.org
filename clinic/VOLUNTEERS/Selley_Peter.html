Volunteer Experiences

CONSULTATIONS GUADALUPE-STYLE
or: what you didn´t learn in Spanish school

OK so you are sat at your desk, raring to go with your first patient.
Read the patient´s record (ficha) first. You will now know their name, sex, age and weight. From their chart you will have an idea about any past consultations: take these with a pinch of salt. Children´s height and weight are plotted on a graph, but this does not take into account their genetic short stature.

So take a deep breath, open the door and call them by their first name (primer nombre) and first surname (primer apellido). Other patients in the waiting area have an interest in speeding things up so will helpfully relay the name around.

Wait for them at the door, greet them with a “Buenos días” or “Buenas tardes” as appropriate and ready yourself for THE HANDSHAKE. You have to be ready to respond appropriately to a grip which may vary from a forceful crunch (rarely), to a mere touching of forearms. More commonly there is just a weak, brief meeting of the two hands. Introduce yourself “Soy Dr ABC, soy de XYZ”. Show them to their seat. All the following commands can be accompanied by a “por favor”.

Start off with “Siéntese”, then move to your seat. You might ask “¿Cuál es su problema?” Always use the “usted” form, (unless you feel particularly confident and want to use “tú” with children)

Most commonly (and you will have clues in the ficha) they will launch into “Me duele…” and list the various parts of the body that hurt. Cabeza, huesos, estomago, ovarios etc. (in children it´s the cabezita, huezitos, etc.).Variations on the “me duele…” theme are “me quema…” and “me arde…” for burning sensations – (“arde” only seems to apply to the gullet), and “me pica…” or “me come…” for itching. Fortunately they will demonstrate graphically with their hands where and how it hurts, burns, itches etc. A minute or so later they will have come towards the end of their list of symptoms and you can chose one or two to explore further. (Note: “de repente” here does not mean “suddenly”: instead “from time to time”.)

If they don´t understand they will look blank and may say “¿Mande?” and you have to repeat or modify your questions.

They may come with a handful of “examines” – test results - and are well versed with “echo, radiografia, tomografia” etc. “Bichos” by the way means worms or other intestinal parasites.

If they complain of burning extremities, they will be thinking kidneys. If they have “mareos” then they believe “colesterol” is the cause.

By now you may have decided to examine them “Voy a examinarle” on the couch “la camilla”.

Despite what the books say the best way to get them on their feet is “Párese”. You may want them to remove a layer or two of clothing with “Sáquese su chompa/camisa/chaqueta” or “pantalon” (not pantalones here). They will usually put their clothes in a pile on the floor: offer them an empty chair. Point them towards the couch and “Acuéstese” will have them flat on their back. If they still have their trousers on then “Abra su correa” will have them undoing their belt.

A thorough examination will usually be normal, when you say authoritatively “Usted no tiene nada grave” which will often give them great relief and they sometimes will tell you they thought they were dying or had cancer. Explain what you think their problem is: that is not their “riñones” but their “musculos”, or “gastritis”. Most are very receptive to the notion that “nervios” may be part of their problem and then will tell you a tale of all their domestic woes.

I believe it is worth explaining that any treatment you give for pain is not likely to “curar” their problem, only “calmar” or “ayudar” and if they find the treatment helpful, they may need to continue with it. Many patients are disenchanted with the lack of effect of “inyecciones” or “ampollas”, that they already have had, be they of “calcium”, diclofenaco”  or “vitaminos”.

Get them back on their feet with “Póngase de pie”, and dressed with “Póngase su ropa”

I think it is helpful to spend some time discussing your diagnosis and treatment (n.b. do not use “discutir” which means “to argue” – use “hablar con”).

You now think you´re done, and that a prescription will get them out of the door. But, no, they will remind you about the 3 or 4 other items on their hit list of complaints. Here you have to use your skill explaining that paracetamol will help all known pain.

So get the prescription written, (getting them out without a prescription is “advanced level consulting”). You may have to say “Es todo” and that they can return “Se puede regresar” if they don´t feel better. If you make a mistake and want to apologize “Disculpe” is more appropriate than “Lo siento” as they might say in Spain.

Invariably the patients are exceedingly polite when it comes to the DEPARTURE RITUAL. There is a minimum of “Gracias, ciao” and a further handshake (see above), but most patients are flattering and effusive in their thanks. “Muy amable doctorcito”; and the doctorita may get a hugs or kisses. Older patients may wish blessings on you and that “El Señor” will help you with your work and “que Díos le pague”. There will be yet more handshakes and you will feel genuinely humbled by their affection and appreciation.

A parting shot could be “¡Que pase bien!”

And on to the next one.

P.S. One more thing: Asking a patient: "¿Entiende?" is not very appropriate. You should ask: "¿Me hago entender?” because if they don´t understand it is probably YOUR fault as you didn´t explain it very well!

P.S. Anatomy of the Back.
“La columna” translates as the spine, from “el cuello” (neck) down. “Espaldas” seem to be the area either side of the spine, anywhere from the shoulder blades to the hips (“caderas”). “La cintura” is the waist.

Most pain in the “riñones” has nothing to do with the kidneys and is probably musculoskeletal in origin. If they have problems with “asientos” it is only indirectly associated with sitting, and means they have a problem with their bowels, usually “estreñimento” or “diarrea”.
