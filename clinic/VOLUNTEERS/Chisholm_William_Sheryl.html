Anesthesia Report for 2011 ENT team

Anesthetist – William Chisholm, CRNA

Surgeon – Burger Zapf

This is an anesthesia report for the ENT mission trip Sept. 12 to Oct. 6, 2011.  First of all, let me state that Amanda is great!  We need to clone her for a few more!

The anesthesia materials are interesting.  I gave Amanda a list of recommended/needed supplies for the upcoming mission teams.  The monitors are marginal.  There is an EKG, but few patches.  There is an O2 Sat Monitor which seemed to work most of the time.  The EKG machine has an auto blood pressure cuff on it, which rarely worked.  We did have a small auto BP cuff which worked well if the patient was not too obese or a pediatric patient.  At that point one uses the mercury hand-pump spigmonomiter .

I had brought a finger probe SaO2 monitor which was our only monitor besides a Nursing Assistant for the recovery “room” (hallway).  Thus the patient needs to be fairly awake when going to the Recovery room.

Tubing for fluids did not have ports for medications injection other than a rubber injection area where the tubing connects to the IV catheter.  Thus bring your own IV tubing with a port or two.

Medications are hit and miss.  Tell Amanda what you want and she will try to get it.  Most medications are expired and /or are a different concentration than we are used to in the United States.  Sevo runs thru an Ethrane Vaporizer which works ok.

I found that the patients are fairly “light weight” when it comes to needing post op pain medication.  Usually one ml. of Fentenyl  intraop works and even that had to be occasionally reversed.  I did suspect that some patients were “pre-medicating” as a couple got no pain medication but still had to be reversed with Narcan post-op.

Overall, most patients seem to be fairly healthy.  Whether that was due to they are healthy or lack of health care to find health  problems is unknown.  We did have problems post-op with a couple and one did have to be sent out to a local hospital, but problems were rare.  Part of this was due to excellent surgeons and great care from Amanda.

But one of the main problems was a shortage of qualified help.  When not doing anesthesia, I was cleaning my masks, tubes, etc (nothing is disposable), helping to clean the room and set up for the next case, watching the patient in recovery and interviewing the next patient.

While doing anesthesia, I also preformed the duties of the circulating nurse as we only had one or nurse to circ and scrub.  Thus I was busy!

But overall, my time at the clinic was enjoyable as it is great to help people who truly need help and it helps when the staff at the clinic are great to work with!
