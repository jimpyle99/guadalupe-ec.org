Volunteer Experiences

Nangaritza Experience 2006

By now you all know that we made it out of the jungle and have already experienced our first civilized frustration with sending an internet account last night.  So here we go again...you will be getting two accounts.  One will be from Jim and the other from me.  Here is Jim's  Remember, these are our journal...so if you have no time remember the location of the delete button.  We thank you all for your good wishes!!

We have returned safely from our all expense paid jungle adventure, only slightly worse for the wear (a cracked rib for me and GI distress for Lauri).  Sunday (April 23) we awoke to pouring rain, finished our packing, and breakfast as our driver arrived.  We hauled all our bags of meds, equipment and a daypack of personal items to the taxi (a pick-up truck) and headed off...a 3 hr. ride (only 15 minutes on paved surface) to the end of the road and the boat dock.  In typical EC fashion, the rest of the group then discovered they had forgotten to pick-up the nurse (who could certainly have been left behind).  So, the taxi returned to Guayzimi, a 2 hr. round trip while we ate lunch in a small cafe (very Ecuadorian).  By the time they returned and the ambulance boat was loaded, it was 1 pm as we started up the river against a pretty strong current.

Initially the river coursed through steep canyon walls with moss covered rock and an occasional waterfall.  This was very pretty.  Aside from the current, travel seemed not too difficult at the start (that would change drastically) except for some serious rain at times (Amazon style) -- fortunately Padre had loaned us a couple of ponchos and the boat was roofed, so we stayed pretty dry.  But... more about the boat.  It was a long, narrow steel affair with seats for about 6 people and we were 10.  The rest sat on bags or stood.  It was powered by a 60 hp outboard.  Since it was the river ambulance, it had an army-like stretcher strapped to the roof and folding supports on one side to attach the handles.

After 1 hr. in the water, we stopped at a village to pick up the Ec Doctor (another waste) and more med supplies and headed on.  A short distance later, we reached a military post (we were on the border with Peru and in what had been the disputed treaty area until about 5 years ago) and stopped for a visit (no apparent reason, we thought).  Then ensued the first of many discussions between the two young women (health providers) and the boat driver about where we were planning to spend the night.  It turned out that one of them had a boyfriend at the base and wanted to stay...hours from where we needed  to work the next day.  They were very willing to tell the villages that we couldn't get there!  Fortunately, Amanda the bilingual nurse from Guadalupe was able to translate all these discussions as well as putting in her-our opinions.  Also they said they had not brought blankets for this 4 day trip!

Finally, we pushed on, all the while losing valuable daylight and then the going got rough with shallow water.  We started with the two young men up front using poles and jumping out periodically to push and pull the boat.  Finally, they let me do the same.  We were all wearing rubber boots which reached almost to the knees but they are really to protect your feet, not keep you dry.  The process involved pulling and pushing the boat sometimes with passengers and occasionally with them walking along the river or through on the jungle path which ran along side the edge while we moved the boat.  Each time I crawled back aboard, I emptied the boots and rung out my socks....why, I'm not sure, since my feet stayed wet for 3 days.

By 5:30 we reached Nuevo Paraiso, a small barrio and after another discussion they elected to stay for the night while the driver would take the boat around a shallow area and be ready for the morning.  (This village was on our schedule, but for 36 hours later).  The drug bags were left on the boat and we carried food plus our knapsacks up the hill to the village ...a 15 minute walk.  Again, after some discussion, they opened the school (a two room affair with a bathroom and plugged toilet with no water).  These schools have windows with bars but never glass.  It was pitch dark by 6:30, but the village had electricity so we had lights as we set up for the night...a thin mattress on the cement floor.  We had each brought a blanket and sheet in our packs.  We ate the lunch we had been given earlier in the day, turned off the light to reduce the bugs flying in, and slept with our clothes  and jackets on (the first to three nights).

I think we were asleep by 8:30 and actually slept pretty well until about 5:45 am when the driver stuck his face through the bars and announced "Buenos dias"...the boat was ready.  Since we wee to return that night, we carried our personal packs (were unwilling to leave them) and headed down a very muddy path to the river on the other side of the village and headed off.  To our surprise, we were served hot chocolate and a roll as we headed up river.  After a short ride, we stopped for a "house  call" to see a 15 yr. old girl, two months pregnant with reported nausea and vomiting.  We climbed the hill to the house and both the EC doc and I examined this young woman with crampy abdominal and back pain .. a pretty much normal exam.  So probably it was musculoskeletal, but later that night I wondered if we could have missed an ectopic )a fatal disease out there).  I happened to have a bottle Aleve in my knapsack, so gave her a few and off we went again.  After about 3 hours of pushing and pulling the boat, we were off loaded on a jungle path and a good 30 minute walk to the village of Yawi.

Rather than setting to work in their health center (lots of dirt, toilet without water, some dirty chairs etc...the health personnel had abandoned their post years ago)  we were welcomed with breakfast.... chicken soup with yucca (not bad).  Finally, we got to work.  I saw 30 people in probably 4 hrs with Lauri and Amanda's help - most needing parasite and giardia meds while the EC doc saw another 20 (hew said).  After finishing, we had to eat again - chicken and rice with yucca again.  Back to the boat which had come all the way to the village and off we went - this time down stream, running the rapids and bouncing over rocks.  This is when, while rounding a fast corner, we crashed into an unseen rock which flung me against the edge of the boat and I was sure I had cracked a rib.  We then stopped the barrio of Yayu and helped the Ec doctor see 10 kids to speed the process so we could get back before dark.

After another trap through the mud, we were back in Nuevo Paraiso and it was quickly dark.  Remember, we are very close the Equator here.  This night the power went out about 7 pm for an hour or so after which we were called to dinner in a house - chicken again with rice while other chickens wandered in and out.  The entire cooking area was lit only with candles and hulled corn was piled behind the table for grinding later - quite a sight!  As the Ecuadorians are shorter, I couldn't fully stand in the house.

We expected another good night of sleep, but it wasn't to be had.  First of all, bats kept flying through the room all night, then someone's radio started playing booming Latin music around 4:30 or 5 am and , of course the dogs and roosters started in.  Sometimes lack of electricity is a good thing!  With being up at 6 am, I somehow thought we could get started seeing patients there by 8 am...but no way.  We finally, after setting up in two different places, we got started around 8:30 or 9 and then had to break for breakfast (same house) of rice with a fried egg on top.  We then worked straight through to 2 pm seeing 30-40 people (meanwhile the EC doc saw maybe 10 and was no where to be seen after noon or earlier.  Did I mention that there were 5 bats flying around the exam room while we examined the cutest 8 mo. old twins.  We also discovered the health center (right next to the school) actually had a toilet that worked (of course locked the first 36 hours we were there).

By 2:30 we had the boat loaded and headed on down river running rapids all the way until we reached the military base again where two rivers joined.  Then, of course, another discussion followed due to the fact that the driver didn't want to go up the other river (saying it was too low and he wanted to go home to a nearby village).  Fortunately, the director of the program which had actually invited our involvement had joined up earlier that day and would have none of this.

Finally we went up the other river with the driver seemingly choosing bad routes and frequently trying all he could to convince us he could go no further.  Again, we pulled and pushed the damned boat through rapids after rapids - can't count the number while the EC doc sat on his ass or walked with the women when we unloaded.  AT one point they walked across the river while holding hands to provide stability against the fast current.  By 5:30 it seemed like we should be getting close to our destination..but oh no.  Another hour of pushing/pulling/walking and now it was really getting dark.  It was very hard to see anything in the water and we were definitely getting nervous.  The landing was made at 6:50 pm.  We could see some faint light in the distance and thought the nard part was no over ---wrong!

In the pitch black, we then climbed up a 45 degree hill in mud up to our ankles with packs and flashlights.  Not all of us had lights so our progress was rather like an inch worm..one person with alight would go ahead a ways, stop light the area for the next one, then go ahead etc.  All the way I was thinking that I was going to topple over backwards and slide all the way down.  It was about this time that I was using every expletive I've ever learned multiple times.  By the time I had fallen once on my sore rib and finally reached the top, I was furious with this entire project.  So began the night to remember...

By flashlight we were guided to one of the 13 houses in the community .. our lodging and dining location for the night.  This very simple house of rough-sawed lumber had 1 1-2 floors with an open middle where there was a hammock and table...everything lit by candlelight - no electricity.  We were shown a room in which to change clothes(I was wet to nearly my waist from the river) and then waited for dinner to be fixed - this time rice and some kind of meat none of us recognized (turned out to be a yamala - a tapir like wild animal.  Hope it isn't endangered!).  It was pretty good except that Lauri and I had just about reached our limit with eating rice.  I should mention that every effort was made to keep our food uninfected and we had bottled water.  Everything was cooked except some fresh apples although the dishes were washed with water flowing down the mountain.

I forgot to mention the place we washed off all the mud...a constantly running hose in the middle of the open central area - also were all the dishes and clothes were washed and people bathed when not in the river.  The special pleasant memory of the evening was sitting by a bonfire and the fantastic display of stars - could nearly reach out and touch them.

Amanda, Lauri and I were then shown to our sleeping space upstairs along a very narrow stairway outside the house to a small room with a bed (no mattress) but a thin pad) and floor space for Amanda.  All the while we were all thinking how we could manage the stairs during the night in the pitch black...there was no visible latrine anywhere.  Thank heavens the flashlight batteries still worked.  Sleep for the 3rd night in the same clothes was fitful at best with either the pigs oinking or having trouble getting comfortable (ribs sore on one side and hip on the other  or bladder calls).  We were up  before first light as the sharpening of machete blades on the stone along side the house was in full swing.   I was hoping for an early start  (did I mention that I-m a slow learner????)

Finally started seeing patients in the school and then had to break for breakfast (rice toped with a fried egg again and plantenos).  First we saw all the school kids and their teacher and then started on the rest of the community..very gracious people most of whom needed parasite, giardia and pain pills for back or head pain.  This day the EC doc did nothing (said there were no sick children)...actually he did see about 9 kids I was told while we saw 60 and finally finished about 3 pm by which time I was furious again ...not with the people but with this idiot doc and nurse (Dr. Do Little and Nurse Do nothing.  Actually, I have different titles, but Lauri won't allow me to use them here).

We loaded our gear, went back down the muddy hill (could now see where we were going  WOW!!! - maybe it was better to do it in the dark) and there the rest were cooling their heels with a swim in the river.  By now I had resolved never to work an EC doc again or to return with similar conditions (lack of clarity of who had the authority to make decisions etc) but we did good work.  The Director (Sonia) was really trying to make things better, but she was and still is fighting an uphill battle.  We later learned that she had asked for the Jornanda from the clinic because she was well aware that the EC docs didn't work hard.

The ride back down the river was as exciting as ever..but no more cracked ribs.  We dropped off the doc (good riddance) at Shaime and they then took us to a jungle hotel run by a young Swedish man and his EC wife...no electricity, but nice beds, a shower (cold) and great food.  How about Swedish meatballs in the jungle?  Our night was interrupted with an Amazonian rainstorm on our metal roof and occasional sprays of water on my face but we at least had a working bathroom.

It's now Thursday morning and we headed back upriver to our final community..with all of the rain, we were treated to multiple waterfalls.  And, at breakfast, the proprietor pointed out a white throated toucan nearby in a tree (one of 2 or 3 species near the hotel).  This day I thought the going would be easier, but wrong again.  No boat pushing, but after landing with our supplies, we walked nearly one hour through mud and water and across logs which literally felt at times like walking on pads of butter. Everything was slippery with the rain.  Oh, we were also climbing most of the time.  About 15 minutes from the village, Lauri slipped off a log bridge and landed in waist deep water (after an entire month, the first time the camera was NOT in a plastic bag, but fortunately it stayed dry).

This community, Miazi,  looked better.  The inhabitants were Saraguro primarily while the others had been Shuar.  It had an outhouse with a flush toilet in the center of the open area, and were building more and the school had evidence of projects completed and a teacher who cared about the kids.  We worked straight through again and saw about 45 kids and adults by 2:15...same who really needed attention for skin problems and parasites.  One mom described worms crawling out of two of her kids noses or mouths while they slept (Ascaris).

We wrapped our gear and headed back across the swamp ..were dripping wet from sweat by the time we reached the boat.  A quick stop at a waterfall for photos and to watch Darwin (a young EC man who was shooting a video) demonstrate vine swinging.  We WISELY turned this opportunity down!  We were then back at the hotel around 4Ñ30 and served a traditional Shuar meal of fresh tilapia (steamed in leaves).  It would have been better if both of us were feeling better GI wise.

A 3 hr. pickup ride over way too curvy roads and we were back at the mission...none too early (8 pm).  Walking across the hanging bridge while once again carrying our medicine packs, seemed almost like coming home.  After hot showers, we spent the rest of the evening regaling the other volunteers and padre about the week's events and were able to laugh at the experience.  Amanda did say that this Jornada was the most difficult she had been on!"  We did a load of clothes, tumbled into bed and slept at least somewhat better than we had for 4 nights.  Friday morning we spent packing and relaxing.

We said our final goodbyes after lunch (the Germans were great to live and work with) and the Padre took us to Zamora where we caught the bus to Loja and on Saturday the plane to Quito.

Jim 

 

Sunday morning thoughts...after reviewing these all night long (rather than sleeping) perhaps the best way to deal with them is to pass them on.  So here goes.

My feeling about this past week was that it would have been difficult but could have been much better had the organization been better.  There is a feeding program in these remote villages where women are paid a small amount to basically run provide child care for the youngest children while the parents work.  This is so they aren't left alone or in the care of children not that much older than they are.  They are to receive supplementary food weekly and part of what we were supposedly doing was dropping of these food "packets".  This was the young women´s responsibility...although as Jim said, they seemed very willing to just decide not to go places and blame it on what ever.  They were not technically part of the medical team, but due to the joint purpose of the group no one had authority until the director showed up.  A major problem.

The communities are a long ways from anywhere.  Amanda had never been this far up river and  none of us had a way of judging what was possible.  We were totally dependent on the boat driver, basically.  The communities were predominately Shuar, among the poorest of the indigenous groups with a lower commitment to education.  The teacher shows up in the community when ever...no wonder the schools looked sooo bad.  I did see one craft project in a corner, however, scale models of hand carved dug out canoes!.  

How we worked:  Jim and Amanda sat close together with a chair for the patient receiving the consultation.  I sat off to the side surrounded by plastic bags filled with all kinds of medications.  We had been told that we should be prepared to see around 500 patients and our responsibility was to see the children in this nutrition program.  The EC doc. was to see the adults but use our meds.  Of course this didn't happen.,..so we ended up hauling more meds than needed.  Once the problems were identified, Jim would say what we needed to treat and I would get them out of the bags, write out the instructions - in Spanish, of course- and Amanda would do the explanations.  After about 20 just hearing the problems was enough for me to get the meds ready.  Sitting on the little benches with grown up seats (ours) gave us another source of discomfort.

Those of you who wish to be free of Hippa (the government privacy act) need to come here.  There was a constant stream of people in and out of the door until we found ways of propping it shut, but this didn't cut down on the line of people peering inthrough the iron grate windows.

I wish you could have seen and I could have caught on a photo the children who would sit and rub Jim's arm while he talked with them and/or their mother.  The Ecuadorian men do not have hairy arms and they petted Jim rather like one would pet a dog.  A cute picture!

When we arrived in a village very few people would be around, but before long there was a child explosion.  Unbelievable how many children women had - sometimes as many as 7 or 8 and many young women of 22 or 23 had 4 children.  I have a MAJOR bone to pick with the Catholic church's teaching on birth control. It is a terrible situation - very cultural.

I am also haunted by the people we couldn't help.  A father telling us about his 16 year old son with osteomylitis (bone infection) who could no longer walk and now had pus coming out of his arm.  He was about 20 minutes away from where we were and we didn't have time to walk there,,,,but then we had no appropriate meds to use anyway.  He had been treated in a hospital in the past but we don't know if he had completed a course of therapy due to the expense.  Now of course who knows...  There was also the young mother with 15 mo old and  pregnant who told us she was having headaches because her husband got drunk and then beat her.  And the 12 year old who sat and cried as we talked with her because her drunk father beat her mother regularly.  All of us were tearing as we listened and had nothing we could do except ask her teacher to help.  She had no other family in the community, her mother wasn´t with her...there is no support system for family violence and it is a big problem here.  As for women who have a difficult delivery...well, they die.

The jungle was green, green and had vines galore.  But I think to best experience it, one needs a biologist-botanist someone who can explain more of what we saw.  No snakes, thank heavens, but we were always on the watch.  The closest was a 6 inch bright red centipede.  The butterflies almost made me want to get out my early childhood net.

It was interesting as we ate together and talked about our return to the US that the director, Darwin and a couple of the other EC on the adventure asked us to take them along in our suitcases.  Getting into the US is very difficult for Latin Americans currently.....

Lauri

Year 2006: Suggestions and Thoughts...

Here are our suggestions/thoughts.  I hope that they are helpful for the continued evolution of the clinic.

We both feel that a medical director, a physician, for the clinic is becoming essential.  How this position would be filled can vary and it is probably the most difficult of our suggestions.  We feel that the clinic is being pulled in many directions i.e. the different kinds of surgeons/surgeries wanting to come with the different requirements for nursing care, medications, and equipment need to be managed.  The clinic can not be all things to all people.   Hard decisions about what will be available at the clinic need to be made based on medical needs, facilities and steady staff.  In order to maintain quality and not over reach what is possible there needs to be an agreed upon vision .  This vision will then dictate what donations are solicited/received in the areas of medications/equipment etc.

If surgeons are at the clinic doing surgeries which require nursing involvement in monitoring the recovery, they - they surgeons - need to bring nurses along with their team.  I do not feel that it is appropriate for Amanda to be given this responsibility along with the other ones she carries.

I would suggest that a basic medical orientation form be developed for the volunteer doctors around issues seen at the clinic for which they might need increased understanding. Ideas include information about the treatment of parasites, amoebas, worms etc.  Medications that are available, dosages, - basically treatment protocols would be helpful.

Information about the complaint of mareo - dizziness which can be the result of dehydration or sustained sun exposure and the fact that this can be helped by increased water consumption in the fields etc.  Volunteer doctors more than likely are not familiar with the kind of work and the conditions for that work experienced by the patients who come to the clinic.

Include information about how to refer patients to medical centers in Loja/Cuenca.  i.e. forms needed/phone calls/ what is the process so that the volunteers know how to get this done when necessary.

Also include information for the volunteers about when Germania is available to fit glasses and how to access her when a patient comes in after Germania has called to see if she has anyone needing her attention.  The volunteers need to know the hours and any dental technician working by the eye glasses place also needs to know how to direct patients whose first stop is that spot - because the see the sign "lentes".

Other medical issues which are present/frequent in the area .

This information could be developed and put in printed form to be handed to the new volunteers. 

The patient health education forms that Ken was working on need to be put to use.  They need to be printed and copies need to be made available in each exam room for the doctors to hand to the patients they see who could benefit from that information.  During the month we were there, these were not handed out and much good can be achieved by using this way of education.  While not all patients can read, the doctor could ask if they would like some printed material which would be helpful, and those who can not read can say 'no".

The physician exam rooms need to be set up with standard supplies and maintained so that those supplies are always available.  This would decrease the time on Amanda's part to track down supplies which are needed and allow the Dr. to be more efficient with less frustration.  Ideas to include are:

a.  Otoscopes and ophthalmoscopes - rechargeable ones - not battery operated
b.  Gloves - sterile and non-sterile
c.  Sterile knife blades
d.  Clear slides for paps and wet mounts.  The current slides are cloudy and need to be discarded. 
And new clear slides ordered.  It is not possible to do accurate paps with the current slides.
e.  Sterile Q tips
f.  Tongue blades
g.  Prescription pads
h.  Pens
i.  English/Spanish dictionary
j.  Dressing supplies   4x4 or 2x2
k.  Tape
l.  Sterile syringes
m.  Xylocaine
There may be other supplies - I'd suggest asking Ken for input.

Orient any volunteer nurses to the location of these basic supplies  - they can be the ones to restock the exam room - as well as where other emergency/basic supplies are available.  This would also cut down on the work Amanda currently does.

Consider purchasing sleeping mats such as therma rest pads for the Jornadas to be used in instances when the medical team is not sleeping at hotels.

I noticed while there that some of the patient records have understandably gotten out of alphabetical order.  This is something that a volunteer could review (a little training/explanation) and put in order.  Would save time for Rita/Amanada in finding previous patient records.

The volunteers need to know how to handle requests for food from Loja.  Specifically they need to know what they can request to be paid for by the mission and what they need to pay for themselves.

This idea needs to be highlighted.    A basic formulary (listing of medications) needs to be developed.  This would include the medications that are used regularly in the clinic.  Parasite meds, diabetes meds, cheap generic blood pressure meds such as HCTZ, Atenolol or other basic beta blockers (Ken could certainly provide input in developing this) etc. which are always available.  These may need to be purchased, but would then be always available to patients and the Drs.  Now patients are started on meds for High blood pressure which have been donated, and when they come back for a refill that med may not be available and they then have to be switched to a different drug which does not work well for stable control. 

There are also many drugs which are currently  not  used, take up space in the pharmacy and out date.  With the development of a clear vision for the kinds of medical care the clinic will provide and taking into account the kinds of problems which the Drs. see on a regular basis, decisions could be made which would identify the necessary meds as well as those which are not needed.  This could then help target the donations as there are some meds which simply aren't needed in Guadalupe but which come and take up shelf space.

If the clinic is ever asked about what donations are needed, I'd suggest a request for toothbrushes which could be given out to the children.

What about free dental care for the children including some basic education about dental care?

Guess this is all for now.  We really saw much progress in the clinic over the two years between our visits.  Evidence of much hard work.  Our greatest concern is that there needs to be a vision about what the clinic can do well and regularly and those needs need to be targeted.  There is a danger in attempting to provide all services to everyone.

Take care.  Hasta luego!

Lauri and Jim
