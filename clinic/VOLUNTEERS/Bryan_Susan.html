Volunteer Experiences

ANAESTHESIA AT CLINICA MISIONAL, GUADALUPE-ECUADOR

SUMMARY OF THE WORK DONE 2005 

During a 13 day stay (March 5 to March 17, 2005) at Guadalupe Clinic I provided anaesthesia for approximately 30 patients undergoing a variety of general surgical procedures.  I accompanied a Canadian surgeon, Dr. Dan Mahoney, to the clinic to do this work.

I provided 19 spinal anaesthetics, 9 general anaesthetics and 2 local anaesthetics with intravenous sedation.  The commonest surgical procedures performed were inguinal hernia repairs with mesh, umbilical hernia repairs, and epigastric hernia repairs.  In addition, several minor plastic surgical procedures were completed (excision supernumerary breasts bilateral axillae, excision large cheloids ear pinna, excision granulomatous lesion ankle).  A large parotid tumour was excised under endotracheal general anaesthesia.  An acute traumatic amputation of 4 fingers resulting from a mining accident was debrided and repaired under general anaesthesia.

No major complications resulted from the anaesthesia and surgery provided.  One elderly male patient with pre-existing prostatic problems was unable to void post-op following spinal anaesthesia for hernia repair.  He required overnight urinary bladder catheterization.  The catheter was removed the next morning by which time he was able to void freely and was discharged.  Several patients developed low grade fevers during the first 24 hours following surgery.  Febrile patients were kept an extra day in the post-op short stay area and received an extra day of intravenous antibiotic medications following surgery.  No overt post-op infections were encountered.  No other post-operative anaesthesia complications were noted.

PREOPERATIVE ORGANIZATION

Prior to our arrival in South America, the Mission made a series of local radio announcements about the visit by a surgical team to the clinic and invited patients to attend.  This seemed to be the best way to get the word out of the new service.

Preoperative patients were screened by the surgeon first.  Those requiring relatively low-risk procedures that could be performed either on an outpatient basis or in a short stay unit (one or two nights maximum overnight stay) were selected by the surgeon and slated into an upcoming surgical schedule over the next 10 days.  These patients were then reviewed by myself for anaesthesia issues/risk and any preoperative testing that might be needed (hemoglobin, BUN, creatinine, liver function tests, urinalysis, glucose and do-it-yourself EKG all available at the clinic).  A modest knowledge of medical Spanish was essential for the anaesthetist to review these patients (only one English/Spanish speaking nurse was available to help with translations and she was needed most by the surgeon).  After assessment by the surgeon and by the anaesthetist, patients were sent home with instructions to return on the day of surgery with an empty stomach.  They were asked to shower prior to coming to the clinic on the day of surgery, and to arrive with a relative who was willing to stay overnight with them at the Clinic (to feed and help care for them postoperatively).

THE DAY OF SURGERY

Although outpatient surgery is quite feasible for most patients having minor operations such as hernia repairs in Canada, this approach was not reasonable at the Guadalupe Clinic.  The Clinic is not road accessible and therefore some patients had to walk long distances and catch several buses, or ride a horse often for some hours to get back home after surgery.  Conditions at their homes were not ideal for outpatient safety (no telephone, many hours from medical help, often no clean running water).  The alternative of having patients stay at a small hotel in the nearby village of Guadalupe was considered, but the hotel was about half a kilometer walk from the clinic (across a bridge over the river) and also rooms in the hotel were up three flights of stairs which were too difficult for the patients with recent hernia repairs to climb.

For these reasons, all hernia repair patients were kept in the post-operative recovery area for at least one night.  This allowed us to monitor them for fever or other problems, to provide them with analgesic medications, and also to routinely provide several doses of post-op intravenous antibiotics to prevent infection.  The clinic hired a nurse to monitor the patients in the recovery area over night and administer their medications.  Most patients were ready for discharge by the following morning.  Those with persistent low grade fever were kept on IV antibiotics for more than a day.  Hernia patients were discharged home with a small supply of Acetaminophen with Codeine tablets and a short course of oral antibiotics.

The one room Recovery Room was a busy and congested location.  No meals are provided by the Clinic to patients, so most patients brought a relative with them to provide food, drink, and personal care.  On arrival on the day of surgery patients were assigned a bed in the Recovery Room and then sent to the shower to scrub head to toe with a Proviodine (or Chlorhexidine) surgical scrub package.  They were provided with a clean patient gown and an IV of 1 litre of Normal Saline was started.  Although 7 beds were present in the Recovery Room, only 5 were accessible to patients as the other 2 were jammed in corners (these were used by sleeping relatives of patients at night).  During the day there were usually still some patients in beds from the previous day’s operations (waiting for buses to ride home, or horses to arrive) and meanwhile a new group of patients was arriving to get showered and gowned prior to the current O.R. day.  There is a single washroom with toilet and shower available for all these patients and relatives.  Male and female patients all share the same room with little privacy, but seem used to such a set up and appeared quite content and comfortable.

The operating room itself is a large clean room, quite new, with good overhead lighting and a good mobile surgical spotlight mounted overhead.  It has no windows, which aids cleanliness, but consequently the room is not well ventilated and tends to get hot and steamy.  Power supply is reliable because the Clinic has a back-up generator which can be started reliably by the staff whenever there is a power failure (happens quite often).  Three fully functioning steam autoclave units are available next to the operating room area for sterilizing equipment.

ANAESTHESIA EQUIPMENT

Equipment available to the anesthetist at the time of my visit is outlined below:

1) Narkomed Compact Anaesthesia Machine  + Drager AV Ventilator and Absorber

- I placed a photocopied “Specifications and Equipment” guide and Instructions Manual for this machine (all in English) in the anaesthesia machine drawer for future reference

- the  “Narcomed Compact” is by North American Drager Company, 148B Quarry Road, Telford, Pennsylvania. USA. 18969.  This machine is Drager Ave Serial #4886.

- ventilator has a hanging bellows and the machine has a rate and volume control to adjust minute ventilation.  Volume adjustments are made manually with a “push-bar” mechanism right on the bellows assembly.

- a technician from the nearby hospital in the town of Zamora checked over and apparently serviced the anaesthesia machine a few weeks before our visit.  Obviously there is no one on site to service or maintain the machine regularly and this is therefore the visiting anaesthetist’s responsibility.

- at the Clinic there are a number of sets of light weight plastic anaesthesia circuit tubing for the “circle  system”.  There are about 7 of these sets in adult size and one set of pediatric tubing.  These are meant to be disposable single use sets;  however, we safely reused them repeatedly by placing a small in-line disposable heat and moisture exchanging filter between the circuit and the patient airway.  The small filters were discarded between each patient.  A small supply of the filters were left at the clinic, but any visiting anaesthetist should probably bring along some new ones unless you plan to clean, disinfect and dry the circuit hoses between each patient.  

2) Oxygen Supply

- there is no piped-in oxygen and no small back up oxygen tanks on the yolks on the anaesthesia machine itself.  All oxygen came from large stand alone compressed oxygen tanks (44.8 kilogram tank size) which were lined up along the wall in the operating theater.  Six tanks were available at the time we were there and had been brought in by truck especially for our visit.  We used up 2 tanks during our 8 days of surgery at the site.  You must connect the anaesthesia machine to one of these tanks (2 pressure reduction valves with on-off hand turning valves and pressure guages and a big wrench were available to connect the tanks up to the valve assemblies and oxygen gas line going into the anaesthesia machine).  Please note that the oxygen pressure must be turned up higher than 28 on the oxygen tank dial to have sufficient drive pressure to run the ventilator.  Below that pressure, the oxygen will flow through the circuit to the patient allowing spontaneous breathing or hand bagging, but the ventilator will not function.

- one oxygen tank was moved to the Recovery Room area temporarily for one patient who required oxygen for an hour postoperatively via nasal prongs.  Otherwise oxygen (and suction) are not available in the Recovery area so all patients were kept in the operating room until awake and stable enough to go to recovery without supplementary oxygen/suction needs.

- there is no nitrous oxide available here presently and it was not needed (it is rarely used now at the hospital where I work in Canada anyway).  The machine is set up to use nitrous oxide (has a flow meter and a nitrous oxide intake line) should this gas be required in future.  

3) Volatile Agents – Vapourizors

- the anaestheia machine has two vapourizors for volatile agents.  However as no volatile agents were available at the clinic, and purchase price of these agents is prohibitively high in Ecuador, I did not use any volatile agents in my anaesthetics.  I was unable to transport any volatile agents aboard the aircraft when I flew to Ecuador as they are on a list of “Dangerous Goods” that the airlines will not allow on board.

- the vapourizors were both quite ancient but probably would work (I don’t believe they have been recently checked and calibrated however).  They work on the direct pour fill method (i.e. they do not fill with pin-indexed colour-coded spigots like modern vapourizors.  You just pour the volatile liquid directly into a small open reservoir on the side of the vapourizor and it runs down a trough into the tank inside the vapourizor.  This obviously leaves room for deadly error if the wrong volatile agent is put into the vapourizor chamber).

- Halothane liquid and a Halothane vapourizor were available at the nearby hospital in Zamora so it must be possible to obtain this volatile agent in Ecuador.

- Vapourizor specifications were as follows:

a) “Isoflurane Vapor 19.1” Serial #61146, made in Germany by Dragerwerk AG Lubeck

b)  “Enflurane Vapor 19.1” Serial #59684, made in Germany by Dragerwerk AG Lubeck

Because no volatile anaesthesia agents were available, I chose to use local, or regional (spinal) anaesthetics when possible, or Total Intravenous Anaesthesia (TIVA) using Propofol for my general anaesthetics.  The clinic happened to have a good stock of Propofol 1% 20 ml glass vials that I could use for this purpose (however, this may not always be the case so check on availability of this drug at the time of your visit, or plan to bring your own).  There is not an infusion pump available at the Clinic for giving TIVA so you should bring your own.  I borrowed a Bard Propofol Infusion pump for this purpose from my base hospital in Canada and returned it to Canada at the end of my stay in Ecuador.   The pump is relatively small and portable and easily carried in luggage.  The anaesthesia machine was used just to ventilate patients with 100% oxygen while the anaesthesia agents were all administered intravenously.

If you do manage to get volatile agents in the O.R. here in the future, be aware that there is no gas scavenging system available and that ventilation of the operating room is not great;  therefore be prepared to be breathing some fumes if you choose to give general anaesthetics with an inhaled volatile agent here.  

4) Alarm Systems on the Ventilator

- the original ventilator machine has a 60 second alarm delay for disconnect/failure to ventilate built in to the machine

- two further safety alarms have been added later to the machine.  One is an audio and/or visual alarm which alerts the anaesthetist to disconnect of the circuit or low drive pressure in the oygen input pipe (i.e. if the oxygen tank is running out, or not turned up high enough to drive the ventilator bellows).  The other is an audio/visual alarm which signals:

a) subatmospheric pressure below negative 10 cm H2O

b) high pressure (above 63.5 cm H2O)

c) sustained high pressure (above 18 cm H2O for 10 seconds)

d) automatic ventilation pressure below preset level (frequency) for 15 seconds

 

5)  Patient Monitors

a) a Spacelabs model 90603A (plug-in, not portable) has the following capabilities:

 - ECG – 5 lead – wise to bring your own “Red Spot” or other monitor buttons for use with this.  It has a good view on the monitor screen and a “freeze” capability.  You can print out paper strips supposedly but I was unable to get the print function to work.  Shows the pulse rate in big numbers on the screen.

- pressure transducers X2 for use with arterial or central lines.  I didn’t need these at all while there and they likely are not something you will be wanting to set up in this setting.

- finger pulse monitor (not an oximeter unfortunately, just a pulse monitor) really didn’t add any new information as ECG was already being monitored.  This pulse monitor could be used on the second (back up) monitor of the same make that was available for use in the recovery room if needed.

- ports for temperature probes to be inserted X2, but no probes available.  Not essential anyway as basic electronic thermometers are available here.

- attached Spacelabs model 90651 automatic blood pressure cuff.  This initially worked intermittently when I arrived, but then the cuff tore, and then finally the BP monitoring machine itself died.  With a new cuff, and repairs to the automatic BP machine this could again be functional.

Thus this main monitor at the present time is only providing EKG pattern and a pulse rate.

 

b) Blood Pressure cuffs

- two small plug-in automatic BP cuffs were used once the better “Spacelabs” automatic cuff died.

- these are plug in electrical units and would give an automatic digital reading only when the “start” button was pushed.  There was no method to set them to record BP every 3 minutes and there were no alarm functions available to alert the anaesthetist to low or high pressure situations.  Also the hose between the patient and the monitoring device was extremely short so the device had to be taped onto the pillow beside the patient’s head in order to reach down to their arm.  Still these units did the job and seemed to give reliable readings (as long as someone remembered to push the button and look at the result every 3 minutes)

- numerous manual BP cuffs and stethoscopes were available, including one pediatric sized cuff suitable for 1 to 5 year old children

- no automatic pediatric BP cuff was available

 

c) Pulse oximeter

- one only battery operated stand-alone finger probe was available.  No ear probes.

- the unit worked well, but had only visual and no audio function.  Therefore the anaesthetist had to remember to look at the small digital screen right on the probe itself every few minutes to check the patient’s oxygen saturation.  As the patient’s finger was often down under the drapes somewhere out of view this was not a very convenient method of watching oxygen levels, and certainly with no audio function or alarms on it, the usual auditory clues of a dropping oxygen level were not present.

- an advantage of this small unit was that it could go with the patient to recovery room and remain on them there for a half hour or so post-op until it was needed again in the operating room for the next patient

 

d) No end-tidal Carbon Dioxide monitor available.

 

6)  Crash Cart

- a crash cart containing a variety of emergency medications, an ambu bag, face masks, oral airways, endotracheal tubes, intravenous catheters

- adult laryngoscope with standard sized MacIntosh blade and also a set of straight blades including two different pediatric sized straight blades

- no ECG monitor on the crash cart .  You would need to hook the patient up to the Spacelabs monitor on the anaesthesia machine, or to the electrocardiogram machine to get a view of the hear rythm.

- several defibrillators at the Clinic, but none of them working at all (lights won’t even come on when plugged in).

 

7)  Intravenous supplies

- a good supply of 1 liter intravenous fluid bags and intravenous catheters, 5 IV poles

- a few central line kits should these be needed in an emergency

- a few bags of volume expanders (Pentaspan, Hetastarch)

- no transfusion capacity here

- no fluid warmers or pressure pumps for infusing volume rapidly

- one patient warming blanket (hardly needed in this climate!!!!)

 

8)  Suction

- no wall suction

- two portable plug-in electrical suction pumps in OR (one for anaesthesia and one for surgeon) with modest suction ability.  These have washable reusable 1.3 litre glass suction bottles on them and are called Schuco Vac by Airco-Ohio Medical.

 

9)  Drugs/Anaestheia Equipment

In general, the Clinic has a well-stocked general pharmacy, but visiting anaesthetists should check on the current availability of anaesthesia drugs as these are often not there at all, or are in short supply.  Many of the drugs are now out-dated but for most uses, this probably doesn’t matter much.  You should plan to bring most of what you need with you.   This includes drugs (particularly muscle relaxants, neostigmine, glycopyrrolate, midazolam, propofol) and specific disposable anaesthesia equipment (spinal needles, endotracheal tubes, sterile gloves, any special sized needles or syringes). .  Muscle relaxants in particular are hard to come by, expensive for the clinic, and out-date quickly so you should bring lots with you.

 The Clinic has suitable sharps disposal containers (all sharps are then burned and buried in a deep well with metal cover outside the Clinic).  Disposable drapes, bloody sponges, body parts, etc. are incinerated in a small outdoor incinerator.  Pathological specimens are generally not sent off for examination unless it is important to have a specific diagnosis.  We did send one tumour off to be examined, but apparently results are not easily or reliably obtained.  Specimens seem to go astray sometimes.

 

SUMMARY

This is a very pleasant and safe place to visit and to work.  Accomodation for visiting physicians is terrific.  Meals are all provided and are delicious.  The setting is truly beautiful and there are excellent opportunities for hiking, bird-watching, and visiting interesting little villages that are far off the beaten track.  There are no tourists here.

The medical facility is generally clean and modern by Ecuadorian standards.  Staff at the Clinic are very helpful and anxious to facilitate your work there.  They appear to have a strong network of support providing a steady stream of supplies, drugs, equipment, and personel from abroad.  The local people are friendly and appreciate the care provided.  The surgery provided at this Clinic is really filling a need for these people as there are few other options available to them that are affordable and geographically near.

Our visit here was the first attempt to provide general surgical and anaesthesia services at this site.  I would say it was most successful and I would feel confident in going there again to do the same sort of work.  

 
Anaesthesia wish list:

Some sort of monitor that includes hopefully all the usually required modalities that anaesthetists monitor during anaesthesia all in one machine. This would therefore have:

a) pulse oximetry with continuous both audio and visual read out of the result as well as alarms that can be set on the oximeter to ring if the value is too low, pulse too slow or too fast

b) electrocardiogram continuous monitor (with ability to print out a rhythm strip if possible but this printing function is not a basic necessity)

c) an automatic blood pressure cuff with several sizes of cuffs (2 adult sizes and one or two pediatric sizes) which can be set to cycle on a regular time frame (every 3 minutes for example) and which can be set to alarm if the blood pressure is too high or too low. The results of the blood pressure reading should appear on the monitor screen

d) an end-tidal carbon dioxide monitor that can be put in the patient circuit to monitor the end-tidal carbon dioxide levels of the patient

The present monitor only offers (b) above at present, and will not print out a rhthym strip. If you cannot get a monitor that offers all of the above functions, then top priority should be (a) the pulse oximeter with audio and visual continuous monitoring capacity. Second priority should be (c) the automatic blood pressure cuff. So far I have managed completely without (d) and managed well enough, but it is usually a standard monitor that is used in anaesthesia.


Furthermore:

A vapourizor of some sort (?Halothane, or preferably Sevoflurane) for which you would need to have a modest stock of the appropriate volatile agent. There is no use having the vapourizor if the agent is unavailable. Also the vapourizor would have to be installed on your anaesthesia machine. This vapourizor probably would not be used very often, as intravenous anaesthesia agents are likely simpler and cheaper to use in your setting. However the volatile agents are generally preferred for small children and infants. Also there may be some anaesthetists who simply are unfamiliar with total intravenous anaesthesia (TIVA) techniques and are only comfortable using inhaled anaesthetic agents.

A peripheral nerve stimulator (used by anaesthetist to determine the degree of muscle paralysis following a dose of muscle relaxant).

A defibrillator for the crash cart that actually works - you have several machines there but none of them seem to be working at the moment.


Experience of the medical brigade en March 2006 

During the two weeks of my stay in Guadalupe in March 2006,  I gave anaesthesia for about 40 operations performed by Dr. Dan Mahoney, a General Surgeon from Chatham, Ontario in Canada.  This was my second 2 week visit to the Clinic to give anaesthesia.

The commonest procedure we did was hernia repairs (inguinal, umbilical, ventral, epigastric), often with mesh repairs.  We also removed several parotid gland tumours, some lumps and bumps, incision and drainage abcesses, repair of a traumatic hand amputation, one full mouth dental extractions on a small child.  The biggest cases we performed were this year when we did two open cholecystectomies (there is no laparoscope there). The gallbaldders were done under full general anaesthesia with narcotic, muscle relaxants, and IV Propofol infusion using an infusion pump.   I did all the inguinal hernias, other than small children,  under spinal anaesthesia.  The small children I did under general anaesthesia using a Propofol infusion pump.  The upper abdominal hernias were done under local anaestheisia with IV sedation  (Versed or low dose Propofol infusion), or full general anaesthesia using Propofol infusion, intravenous narcotics and Rocuronium as a muscle relaxant where needed for the large hernias.

There are no inhaled agents available at the clinic.  They have vaporizors on the anaesthesia machine but no volatile agents available.  If you are able to bring some volatile agents with you (Forane, Ethrane) you could use these I presume, but I was unable to bring these agents aboard the airplane as they are on the list of dangerous/prohibited products for air transport.  There is no nitrous oxide on the anaesthesia machine, and no air mix available.  Therefore all general anaestheisa was done with 100% oxygen plus IV drugs.  The oxygen comes in huge tanks bolted to the wall and is used not only for the patient to breathe, but also to drive the ventilator bellows.  There is no gas scavenging system, but as there are no gases except oxygen, it really doesn't matter.  If you look at the information I have posted on the web page from my visit last year you will have all the particulars on the gas machine and monitors that were available at that time.  I left an instruction manual (photocopy obtained from the company) for the machine in the drawer of the anaesthesia machine.  The clinic has since improved their monitoring capabilities and has two automatic blood pressure cuffs (although they are only "automatic" if you push the start button - they will not cycle every 3 minutes).  They also have a new pulse oximeter which works quite well, two functioning EKG machines which will print out a strip or full cardiogram with interpretation, and also an EKG monitor on the anaesthesia machine itself.

They have improved the physical space by installing a dehumidifier in the operating room which makes it much easier to work comfortably without dripping sweat on the patient wound, and also an exhaust fan in the sterilizer room which has cut the steaminess considerably.  They also have installed curtains between the 6 hospital beds in the overnight ward room so there is some privacy for the patients.  The Clinic managed to provide a nurse to stay with the patients overnight although this was a bit of a challenge at times, with Amanda having to fill in a lot of the night shifts herself while still working all day as well.

We initially had planned to do outpatient surgery for the hernias, but on seeing the living conditions of many of the patients (living miles away up mountainsides, having to return home on horseback or hours on the bus, no clean running water or electricity in the homes, miles from any medical help in the case of any emergency), we decided to keep all the patients overnight at least one night.  The gallbladder patients we did on a Friday so they could stay over the weekend and be discharged out by Monday morning to make room for Monday's incoming patients.  We generally did 4 or 5 procedures per day and that kept the inpatient room full all the time.  We ran IV fluids on all the patients for the first 12 hours or so to hydrate them and give a route for 3 doses of IV antibiotics (one intraop and then 2 further IV doses post-op).

It is a great place to work and the people are very helpful.  You should be able to provide safe anaesthesia there for minor to medium degree surgery including pediatric cases down to about 3 or 4 years of age I would say.

Please don't hesitat to write me with further questions.

 

Anaesthesia Experience at Guadalupe Clinic, March 2007

For the third year in a row I worked as a volunteer anaesthetist with a Canadian general surgeon (Dr. Dan Mahoney) at the Mission Clinic at Guadalupe, Ecuador. During our two week visit we completed approximately 35 major cases (requiring spinal or general anaesthesia and overnight admission) in additional to several minor outpatient procedures under local anaesthesia. Hernia repairs (inguinal, ventral, femoral and epigastric) were the commonest procedure done. A number of these were repaired with mesh. Several anal fistulas were repaired, and a number of lumps and masses were removed. One open cholecystectomy was completed at Guadalupe Clinic, and 5 laparoscopic cholecystectomies were done at the hospital in Zamora (public hospital located about one hour away by car) by our team. The oldest patient operated on was 83 and the youngest, a child of 4 years. No serious anaesthetic or surgical complications occurred, including no significant postoperative infections. With this third year of surgery, our team has completed over 100 general surgical procedures under anaesthesia at this location without any significant complications.

Anaesthesia equipment and methods used were outlined in previous summaries of our visits to the Clinic. However, there were some significant improvements in equipment available this year. The anaesthesia machine is now equipped with a new monitor which records automatic blood pressure readings, pulse oximetry, pulse rate and continuous EKG. There is a second plug-in pulse oximeter and pulse monitor available should it be required in the post-op care unit, as well as a small battery operated mobile pulse oximeter for use by nursing staff in postoperative care.

Another improvement is the new piped in wall suction available. The unit is run through a compressor on the roof of the clinic which provides suction to the dental rooms and also two separate suction lines to the operating room. One line can be used by the surgeon for intraoperative suctioning of blood or fluids. The second line can be connected directly to the canisters on the back of the anaesthesia machine to provide suction at the head of the bed for the anaesthetist. In addition, there is still an electrically driven suction with cleanable glass bottles which can be plugged in if required as a back up. A minor caution – the compressor on the roof tends to heat up a lot and therefore staff usually turn it off over lunch hour and late in the afternoon when the dentists are finished working. A reminder may be needed to the Clinic staff not to turn off the suction compressor while surgery/anaesthesia is ongoing in the operating room.

Anaesthesia and Surgery in Zamora Hospital (which is NOT the Mission Clinic of Guadalupe)

For the first time our team decided to do some surgery in the community hospital in Zamora (about an hour by car away). This was arranged because there were many patients requiring gallbladder surgery who would benefit from having laparoscopic rather than open cholecystectomy. The Zamora Hospital has a full set of laparoscopic equipment (CO2 insufflator, television monitor screen, laparoscopic camera and laparoscopic instruments) but unfortunately the surgeons at the hospital have not received training in use of these instruments yet, and therefore the equipment is not used. Also the Zamora Hospital was able to provide overnight nursing care for our patients. We successfully completed 5 laparoscopic cholecystectomies at the Zamora Hospital. All patients were able to go home the day following surgery. We also elected to do one massive recurrent incisional hernia repair as an inpatient at the Zamora Hospital as this lady needed a prolonged inpatient admission following her surgery and this would have tied up our limited beds available at the Clinic.

The anaesthesia equipment available at Zamora Hospital was in reasonable shape but a few cautions are needed. The anaesthesia machine is fairly modern, but the ventilator on the machine is broken. A technician apparently has already tried to fix it without success. Therefore it was necessary to manually “bag” the patients throughout the operations. This ties up the anaesthetist all the time, and for that reason it is preferable to bring along an anaesthesia assistant to do most of the manual bagging of the patients so that the anaesthetist is able to draw up drugs, change intravenous bags, etc. during the surgery. The machine has vapourizors for Sevoflurane and Halothane. I used the Sevoflurane as my primary anaesthetic agent for some of the cases and did the others using a Propofol infusion pump (which I brought with me – not available at the Zamora Hospital). There is no piped in oxygen. It is necessary to crank on the large tank of oxygen mounted on the wall of the room to turn on the oxygen source. There is no nitrous oxide or tanks of compressed air to mix with the oxygen, so all anaesthetics are given using 100% oxygen. They have an EKG monitor, automatic BP cuff, and oximeter, but the end-tidal carbon dioxide monitor and agent analyser were not working. Suction was fine.

The electricity is not reliable. The power went off for some hours while we were there and a hospital back-up generator came on promptly enough. However, once the generator was on, we kept having trouble with the laparoscopic equipment and anaesthesia equipment “cutting out”as a result of the irregular power source coming from the generator. All the equipment was plugged in through “surge protector” boxes. These boxes kept causing all the electronic equipment to turn off in response to surges of power from the generator. In the end, we ended up taking the surge protectors out of some of the circuits and plugging the equipment directly into the wall outlets so that it didn’t keep turning on and off in the middle of the operation.

You should plan to bring all your own anaesthesia equipment and drugs with you to Zamora. This way you will be assured of having what you need, and it will certainly save the patient an expense. Otherwise you will have to go to the hospital store (part of the hospital, located near the Emergency Room) and buy each syringe, needle, IV bag, drug, endotracheal tube, IV administration set, bladder catheters, tape etc. from the store. This cost is usually charged to the patient if they are having surgery at the hospital. The store sells Sevoflurane and Halothane either in small plastic containers (about 30 mls) or by the 250 ml. bottle (about $225 each). If you are operating on a number of patients it is much cheaper to buy a full bottle of inhaled agent and leave the unused portion at the Clinic for the next surgeon to use. If you are able to bring a bottle of Sevoflurane with you to Ecuador (not usually a permitted substance on aircraft however) this would certainly save the Clinic patients a big expense. If you are doing laparoscopic surgery, you need to bring your own surgical clips with you, or you can buy them in packs of 10 at the hospital store. Another caution – when we first visited the store we were told by the person in charge there that it was always open, so that if we needed anything in a hurry during the surgery, we could just send someone down to the store to get it. In fact, the next two times we went to the store there was no one there and it was locked up. Therefore it is likely a good idea to get all your equipment and drugs together before you start a case, because supplies may not be reliably available through the store when you suddenly need them.

There is no recovery room. Patients must be well enough to go directly back to the ward following surgery, so you may wish to wait for awhile in the operating room with any patient who may have airway difficulties or be excessively drowsy. Oxygen prongs are generally available at the bedside on the ward if needed in the immediate post-op period, but monitoring is limited to pulse, blood pressure and temperature in the post-op ward. Each patient going to the ward postoperatively at the hospital must have their own supply of medications (narcotics, anti-emetics, pain pills, IV antibiotics, needles, syringes, alcohol swabs, dressings for dressing changes, IV bags, etc). Usually the patients keep these supplies in a drawer next to the bedside and the nurse administers them to the patient as directed in your physician’s post-op orders. Another hint – write your post op orders in Spanish and use both the generic and the brand name of each drug to try to avoid problems for the nursing staff in following the post-op orders. It is best to leave an exact supply of each needle, syringe and medication required for the care of your patient in the bedside table and count out the supplies with the nurse and a relative if possible to make sure there is enough of each item. If you don’t leave enough supplies, your patient just won’t receive the medications you have ordered.

Operating room staff at the Zamora Hospital were helpful and knowledgeable, even though they rarely have done laparoscopic surgery. Surgical instruments were chemically sterilized by the staff in Cidex and rinsed with sterile saline prior to use. Sterile cloth surgical drapes were provided. Several local surgeons were helpful in assisting at the operations.

General Surgery at Guadalupe Clinic - March 2008

Once again a team from Canada consisting of Dr. Dan Mahoney (General Surgeon) and Dr. Susan Bryan (Anaesthetist) plus three able helpers (Jeremy Bryan, Mike Bryan and Zack Mahoney) provided general surgical services at the Clinic Hospital for a period of 10 days.

Approximately 40 procedures were undertaken under general anaesthesia, spinal anaesthesia, or local anaesthesia with sedation. The most commonly completed surgeries were hernia repairs (inguinal, femoral, umbilical and ventral).  A variety of other minor procedures included debridement and suture of a machete wound, removal of gunshot pellets from an arm, removal of supernumerary fingers, and excision of an olecranon bursa.  No serious complications were encountered.  Patients having more major procedures such as hernia repairs stayed in a temporary ward at the Clinic for one night following surgery.

There were a number of improvements at the Clinic since our last visit a year ago.  This year we enjoyed cooler operating conditions in the O.R. theatre thanks to the newly installed air conditioner.  A larger new steam sterilizer was a great help in preparing all the sterilized instrument trays required.  We were pleased to stay in the newly built family apartment complex located in the nearby village.  This was constructed by the Mission to provide comfortable housing for visiting volunteers.

Surgical Visit to Guadalupe Clinic – January 23 until February 4, 2011

Surgeon – Dr. Dan Mahoney (Chatham, Ontario, CANADA)
Anaesthetist – Dr. Susan Bryan (Thunder bay, Ontario, CANADA)
Other team members – Volunteers Zack Mahoney, Rachel Bryan and Jeremy Bryan

This was the fifth visit by this team of Canadians to the Guadalupe Clinic.  The Clinic provided substantial publicity in advance of our visit in the form of radio advertisements and posters throughout the region.  The advertising was directed particularly to patients with hernias requiring repair and those with skin lesions, or lumps requiring removal.  As in years past consultations with a surgeon were provided and a number of minor surgical procedures were undertaken.

The commonest surgical procedure undertaken was hernia repair.  Inguinal, umbilical and ventral/epigatric hernia repairs were performed generally on a one night stay inpatient basis.  A number of very large hernias, and also recurrent hernias were repaired usually with mesh.  Anaesthesia was most commonly spinal but regional infiltration with local anaesthesia and also general anaesthesia was administered in about a third of the patients where appropriate for the procedure.  Patients were all well enough to leave the Clinic the day after surgery and were seen in follow up a week or two later.

Generally 4 operations per day were undertaken.  A total of 35 procedures were successfully completed in the 10 day visit.  Patients received a bolus of high dose intravenous antibiotics on induction of anaesthesia.  This appears to have successfully prevented any postoperative infections.  No serious complications were encountered.
