  Mission  Report   from January 19 - April 25th 2005

 

        I arrived in Guadalupe on Wednesday Jan 19th, to contribute to the General Medical care of patients attending the clinic  from that date until April 25th,  I did not meet my predecessor nor have any idea of what was expected  of me.  Amanda, the clinic nurse, being out on Jornada, Clinic staff do not speak English.  The first week was difficult trying to learn what drugs are available in the pharmacy, what patients  needs and expectations are in this unique culture, how to sort them out quickly, where to refer problems, and what facilities were available elsewhere,

        Contributions, included examination and assessment of patients every weekday, from 8.00 AM until all registered persons had been seen,  5 - 25  patients were seen each day by  me.  It takes time to sort out their different problems, plus my needing help with translation, and  trying to select the most appropriate treatment from the pharmacy, I did not request any laboratory investigations unless the results made an essential difference to patient management (Amanda already has too much to do) .The general clinics were particularly busy during the visits of the Ophthalmologists. People of all ages attend the clinic, not always with real reasons, occupying  time and resources which should be given to the really sick, about 25 % had any real pathology,  25% were children under 12.

        I assisted with hernia surgery during the visit of the Canadian team, and dealt with the post operative visits of patients, I also gave some first aid treatment to diabetic foot ulcers, wounds needing sutures,  infected lacerations, fractures, head injury, and did patient requested cervical smears, missing the support of the NHS as a  backup.

        Recommendations

        Consultations: the habit of pushing into the consulting room without a number and notes must be discouraged, also returning with more questions once the consultation is finished, and coming in with a relative expecting treatment for all the family, with one registration.

        Pharmacy:  Supplies of basic simple drugs need to be maintained if possible, diclofenac, acetaminophen, doxycline, metformin, digoxin, codeine, ditopax, non steroidal eye drops to mention a few,

        Lab investigations: Unnecessary investigations requested by patients themselves or from other clinics should be discouraged, accepting only those with a proper written request from a Medical Practitioner. The clinic staff have quite enough to do without this.

        Scans: tomograms,  X-rays,  MRI and such like expensive investigations (hormone assays) recommended to patients must be relevant and essential  to their treatment . Most of out patients cannot afford to pay for these,   

        Clinic Personnel: Another trained nurse is needed to help with triage such that Drs time is more effectively used, patients with multiple complaints, those repeatedly coming to the clinic with the same thing when previous advice is not followed, or those treated elsewhere coming with pathology reports and medications could be screened out, including repeat prescriptions,  those wanting cervical  smears, wound dressings, suturing,  wheel chair bound patients needing to be undressed, those physically compromised and the elderly,  might be prepared in another room together with the items needed, so that consultation time could be more  profitably spent on the poor deprived patients for whom the clinic is intended,

        Surgery done must be simple, dealing with potentially life threatening conditions, which can be managed with the existing  facilities and not involving extended inpatient recovery, (no cosmetic procedures) For patient safety,  all operations done with local, sedation and general anaesthetia need a minimum of 2 informed assistants  in the theatre,  one to help with anaesthetic technique (WHO guidelines) and  subsequent recovery,  Resuscitation needs must be sorted out and be immediately available, including suxamethonium, and oxygen for all procedures, and the assistants trained in their use,

        The habit of using untrained personsnotably for ENT, to give so called sedation, valium and ketamine particularly to children  MUST be discouraged, I am very concerned about patient safety and have written to  Dr Iain Wilson who is the President of World Anaesthesia based at Oxford University, for his comments.

        Anaesthetic machine needs modification to be able to use compressed air instead of non available nitrous oxide, and a vapouriser conversion scale for halothane. At present it is just a source of oxygen. Note that general anaesthesia can be given without a machine.  An Oxford draw over system as recommended for use in Third World Countries should be considered or the Glostavent if funds become available,

        Recovery room is too crowded, 4 beds only would allow patient access, a  mobile oxygen source is essential, such as a Puritan Bennett Oxygen concentrator, (which produces 4 litres/ minute  of Oxygen from room air) This  will save on transport of large cylinders with reducing valves from Loja, suction machine, automatic BP and oximeter  are essential  in addition to those used in the theatre, When there is a financial donation  for clinic equipment, these are priority needs  particularly as there are future plans for further surgery, I have contacted ECHO  re the cost of these, cataloguesales@durbin.co.uk, ref  GP  250838. DurbinPLC,180,Northolt Rd, South Harrow, Middlx,

        Ultra sound machine: The clinic would benefit from this, to save patients the expense and time needed to travel to another clinic, (1-2 requests  most days). There would need to be a volunteer able to use it and to train another local person employed by the clinic, perhaps to include triage and anaesthetic assistant duties as mentioned above,

        Research project:  Probably about 50% of adult patients complain of musculo-skeletal problems, including those well heeled with sedentary jobs, perhaps a volunteer staying  in Guadalupe for some time could find out what is behind this, incidence, initiating factors, morbidity, etc,

        World Anaesthesia: I have written an account of the clinic and description of our facilities for surgery for this publication, hoping to interest relevant teams to offer their services,

       
        Final Comment

        The clinic is very well appointed, and functions brilliantly,  a wonderful  facility in the jungle,  It is a remarkable achievement  made possible by the vision of Padre Jorge Nigsch, who  was most helpful and generous  during my time in Guadalupe. It is however a general medical clinic and not a hospital such that its function needs to be clearly defined.

        The residence is luxurious, if sometimes overcrowded, having more facilities than could possibly be expected in this beautiful remote geographical location. It was however an extremely noisy place during my entire stay which detracts from the pleasure of being in Guadalupe. It is impossible for individuals who are not part of a large group or not wanting to join a pre-existing one to get away to follow their own interests. Residents have different needs which should be respected by the others,  particularly with regard to use of the  kitchen, balcony, and  computer, which must be shared. The  video player area  needs to be closed off as it is a major  source of conflict. There will be residents in the future coming alone and who find noise difficult. May I suggest mosquito netting across the windows which can be opened, to keep out a variety of insects.

        Finally I should like to thank the nuns for their great hospitality, delicious food and friendship,  Amanda for her tremendous help in the clinic, input to patient care, translating and giving me information on the local traditions and culture of the people and P. Jorge for inviting me to come here.  It has been an exceptional experience, privilege, and pleasure to be in Guadalupe for over 3 months such that my contribution to the  medical care of the deprived population is hardly adequate.

                                                 Gillian Davies, MBBS, FFARCS (London)
