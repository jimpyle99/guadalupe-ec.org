Experience of Stephanie Motter

 

Upon arrival, we were greeted at the Mission by the Padre, four Sisters, an American nurse and another volunteer.  Padre Jorge Nigsch and the Sisters are welcoming, warm and fun.  I enjoyed spending time with them during my week stay in Guadalupe.  After getting settled in the volunteers’ residence, we checked out the clinic.  My preceptor, Dr. Serle Epstein, was encouraging when I expressed my uncertainty with tropical medicine and the way medicine is practiced in a developing country.  He insisted that “common things are common” no matter where one practices, and indeed he was correct.  Throughout the week I managed patients with common health problems:  hypertension, diabetes, muscular-skeletal problems and a variety of women’s health issues.  I performed ear lavages on patients whose ear canals were impacted with cerumen.  I used the nebulizer to deliver treatments to asthmatics.  And I provided lots of patient education regarding gastritis/reflux disease, urinary tract infections and depression.  I was really impressed with the capabilities of our simple laboratory and with the wide variety of medications that were available from our on-site pharmacy.   I was also amazed by the knowledge and patience of Amanda Anderson, the full time nurse who essentially runs the clinic, and by Rita, a local woman who is responsible for all the daily goings-on in the front office. 

One particular afternoon I had the wonderful experience of accompanying Sister Andrea and another American volunteer to a nearby village.  We took a bus from Guadalupe and hopped off when we got to our stop which was a half-mile trail studded with grazing cows.  We followed the trail to a suspended foot bridge that crossed the river.  Once across, we followed a frontage road for over a mile into the village.  Sister Andrea gathered all the kids and commenced a youth group where- in what seemed like a world away from Connecticut- I played a version of the same games and sang a version of the same songs from my own childhood over 20 years ago.  The day ended with a game of soccer where we played girls against boys, and the girls won!

I arrived at the mission clinic as a nurse practitioner student in my final year of training.  I left the mission clinic having had the time of my life.  I made several new friendships with both the more permanent folks at the mission as well as with other volunteers who each had a unique story and reason for being there.  Likewise, I developed a greater sense of self-confidence regarding my clinical skills, and I learned to trust my judgment related to patient care…

I absolutely hope to return to Guadalupe again.  If you are considering a trip there to volunteer your time and skills, please seriously consider three things: 

1.  Learn as much Spanish as you can.  Even if you are not fluent or even proficient, any little bit helps. 
2.  Work closely with Amanda for at least one day while you are in the clinic.  Her awareness of the local resources and culture is truly amazing, and working with her added to my experience. 
3.  Attend mass or accompany the Sisters to the villages (no matter what your own religious faith) because it is a good way to get better acquainted with the local people, their traditions and their way of life.  Likewise, it allows the chance to take in the incredible views and sights that seemed to be worthy of photographing at every turn.
