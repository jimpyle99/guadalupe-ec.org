Our experience of the medical brigade...

 

Having seen Guadalupe Clinic’s website, I was not surprised at the bridge we crossed to get there, or at the clean, inviting surroundings.  What did catch me off guard was the hospitality of the sisters and Padre Jorge Nigsch.  It was as if we were all that was important in their day.  We arrived just in time for lunch, and were to leave in a few hours for the first destination of our Jornada:  Chicana.  We had warm welcomes and good turnouts at most of our destinations.  What is surprising is how healthy the people are.  In our 6 locations and roughly 600 patient visits, there was only a handful who really needed help.  Many of them had, in fact, already seen a doctor.  The most common complaints were headaches, heartburn, low back pain, and body pain (dolor del cuerpo).  We gave out very few antibiotics.  I felt much better about the emotional connections we made than about the actual medicine we did.  The people were so friendly and for the most part appreciative.  One of the most connective times was our home stay with a family of 5 in Chicana who all piled into one room with twin beds while each of 2 American couples got their own room.  Despite language and cultural barriers, these people were welcoming, trusting, and so very kind.  I have no way of knowing how they received our medical advice or whether they took the meds we gave them.  But I do know that they had the undivided attention of an American doc, and a fairly complete exam, all for $1 and I feel good about that.  If I were to return, I would make more of an effort to support the existing medical system:  the government clinics and the medicos who run them.  We could learn from them about the indigenous diseases and herbal remedies, and they could learn some of our western knowledge and approach.

I would be happy to communicate further with anyone who’s interested.

Lauri Costello
