Experience

I spent a terrific and unforgettable week and a half at the Mission
Clinic in November 2005. This was my first overseas work experience, an
endeavor I strongly feel I was led to undertake. In making the decision
to do so, the information on the excellent web site along with many
e-mail and personal contacts with Dr. Serle Epstein were extremely
helpful. I was attracted to this location as a place for a short term
assignment for a few reasons. I liked the fact it was on a Catholic
mission. [Although this was important to me, it was less so or
completely unimportant to most of the other volunteers who were there
during my stay. The atmosphere is extremely ecumenical. Participating
in some of the religious aspects of the experience such as mealtime
prayers, mass, etc. seemed interesting and meaningful even to those who
were not Catholic.] I also liked the fact that an established clinic
was up and running on a continuous basis.  I chose the weeks I did
since I felt it would help my adjustment on a short stint to have
another physician already experienced in Guadalupe during my stay. As
it turned out, I had two other physicians during my two weeks. On one
hand, this kept the volume quite low for any one of us on any
particular day (about 10/day/physician). On the other hand, the
camaraderie and opportunity to discuss cases was extremely stimulating
and enjoyable. Working with and getting to know the other two docs,
Kerry Jones and Michael Alber, definitely provided some of the high
points of the experience for me. Amanda Anderson is absolutely
invaluable in the clinic and, in retrospect, I believe that even a
short term stint would be quite manageable by oneself if Amanda is
available. Especially, if one has a good working knowledge of Spanish.
I would emphasize what is stated on the web site, which is that
knowledge of Spanish is very important.

As a family physician, I found the patients were not dissimilar to
those I see in the states. Before going, I re-acquainted myself with
some of the tropical diseases I hadn't read about since med school -
leishmaniasis, bartonellosis, dengue, etc. As it turned out, I didn't
see any of those but lots of typical primary care problems - headaches,
depression, menstrual and gyn problems, musculoskeletal complaints,
etc. I saw some things very commonly here that are seen, but are less
common in the states (USA) such as pterygia and parasitosis. There were
also fair numbers of unusual and interesting problems. In the week and
a half I was there I saw (among my patients and the other docs) a large
thyroid mass, a patient with probable Sheehan's syndrome, a large
baker's cyst, a third degree AC sprain (one year old but never treated)
in a 46 year old woman who was G18P16, probable reoccurrence of TB, 
and a partial rupture of the pectoralis muscle presenting as a chest
wall mass.

Although this was technically a "working vacation" it was as much or
more recharging than a purely recreational vacation. The pace is
relaxed, the people (Padre Jorge, the hermanas, Amanda and the clinic
staff, and the other volunteers) are extraordinary, the work is
stimulating and interesting, and the setting is absolutely magnificent.
The facilities are outstanding for those of a developing country. The
clinic is well stocked and set up and the volunteers' residence is
wonderful. The photos taken from the residence do not do complete
justice to the extraordinary setting. One of my favorite impressions
from the trip is sitting on the terrace looking down a stunning emerald
green valley between mountain ranges with myriads of brightly colored
birds singing and flitting about. Remarkably peaceful!

I would be happy to speak or e-mail with anyone considering spending
some time at the clinic.

Joe
