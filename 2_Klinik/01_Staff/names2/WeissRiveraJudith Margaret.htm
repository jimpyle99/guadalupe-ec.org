My excuse, or Tutupalli

           The reason that I could not attend the last meeting is that I fell off a mule.  I had just returned from Ecuador the night before the meeting. Four days earlier, on a steep, muddy trail, I fell over the front of a mule onto my head.  I sustained a seven inch scalp laceration and a large hematoma.  Fortunately, the hematoma was outside my skull and my neck, though still too stiff to allow me to sleep on my stomach without suffocating, did not break.  I am doing much better, and I hope to make it to the next meeting. 

          Why was I perched on the back of a mule on a wooden rack covered with a folded blanket as I descended a muddy, boulder strewn path just 40 degrees from perpendicular?  I was leaving Tutupalli the sixth day of a medical jornada.  We, that is three doctors, a dentist, a nurse, and various helpers, were traveling to see patients in various remote communities on the eastern slope of the Andes, dragging our equipment and pharmacy with us.  Lucky for me, the dentist had two leftover vials of lidocaine and 3 sutures, and one of my colleagues had done extensive moonlighting in an ER.

          Tutupalli was lovely. It lies in a beautiful valley at 7500 feet.  Most of the inhabitants are Saraguri, an Andean indigenous group.  It is semi tropical; they grow bananas and yucca for personal use, and some coffee, but it is too high for mango, avocado, tamarindo.  Tutupalli is built on a small point above the convergence of two rushing mountain rivers.  Due to the lack of either road or navigable river, the surrounding hills have not been subjected to the widespread deforestation of the Andes.  There is no way to get the wood to market.  Small sections of mountain sides have been cleared to graze cows, but there are no large herds.  Looking up one would often see the strange sight of a Holstein tethered precariously at the top of a steep green mountain top. 

          Unless there are heavy rains with their ensuing landslides, it no longer takes a four hour hike or mule ride to reach Tutupalli. The new road has shortened the trip to one and a half hours.  Once we arrived at the end of the road, I cleverly volunteered to walk down the steep trail to Tutupalli, as there were not enough mules to carry all of us and our equipment.  Though it took me longer to walk down than it took the men from town to carry me up two days later, it was a pleasant walk.  Steep, muddy, hard on the knees, with abundant evidence of mule traffic: still lovely.  Entering the valley was like entering Shangri La.  To the west a huge double waterfall poured down the green mountainside from a height of 10,000 feet.  Though too distant for us to hear it’s roar, the view was our constant companion on the way down.  The intense green of the valley was crowded with brilliant purple trees.  Swarms of iridescent blue and black and orange and black butterflies decorated the path; they flowed ahead of me in a knee high cloud when my rubber boots disturbed them.  I now understand how Gabriel Garcia Marquez imagined his plague of butterflies.  He merely embroidered a bit on reality. 

          Undoubtedly my face was red when I reached the town, after climbing  the brief uphill stretch from the bridge.  Some one generously handed me one of the last bottles of water in town. We briefly examined the clinic before going to eat the lunch that had been prepared for us.  Fortunately, I had dirtied my scrubs when I slid on the trail on the way into town, so there was no choice but to bathe in the river before beginning work.  The second river was a rushing torrent edged with Volkswagen sized boulders.  Several huge boulders were conveniently grouped to form a protected sandy beach. We arrived just as a group of smiling boys were leaving, lugging plastic baskets of clothes they had washed.  School was out so that the teachers could go home to vote in the upcoming election. The boys decided we were the best show in town–they had not yet seen our young German dentist in her bikini– and retraced their steps to watch us swim. From the water I watched families walking on the path above the other side of river, trailing mules and children, then crossing the suspension bridge down river into town to consult us.

          Tutupalli was a town of 78 people, with many more living in the surrounding countryside.  Most were subsistence farmers.  The town has a center square, a few muddy streets, a school, a locked church, and a government clinic.  A paved basketball/volleyball court occupied the main square, the only pavement in twenty-five miles.  The nearest pavement was another basketball court in the town down the road.  The closest paved road was at least 50 miles. The street surrounding the main square was that in name only.  Impassable for any wheeled vehicle, it had a steep ten foot drop off between the clinic side and the guest house side, and contained many hassock sized boulders.  Clearly it was designed with foot or mule traffic in mind.

          The majority of the people were Saraguris, with a few mestizo families who had founded the town several generations before. Many of the older people did not speak Spanish, while most of my contemporaries spoke it as a second language.  The Saraguri men were attractive: fit, self confident, polite.  They wore their hair in a long braid hanging between their shoulder blades.  They wore black trousers cut off just below the knee to accommodate the ubiquitous rubber boots.  The women wore several layers of full black skirts, no longer hand woven but machine embroidered; white blouse, two braids, and a small black bowler hat. Surprisingly, this lush green valley was less fertile than the more barren appearing highlands.  Much of the population migrated up to the higher altitudes for four months a year to grow a crop of mote (Andean corn) or beans, or a cash crop of tree tomatoes.  Rather than taking the three-bus, twelve hour trip by road, most walked with their families over the Cordillera, carrying what they needed on mules.  The priest told me they loaded the mules high with feed sacks containing their worldly goods, tied a crate of chickens on one side, a crate of cuyes-guinea pigs-on the other, and topped it off with the guaguioto, or baby. 

          In Tutupalli, the diet was high in starch, low in protein.  No one was fat,  We ate mote, the large white Andean corn, and yucca fried with onions and eggs.  Most local people got their protein from homemade cheese, and occasional eggs and beans.  Cheese was a practical way to preserve milk without refrigeration, and to make it digestible for those without lactase.  No one would butcher a cow to eat, though if necessary they would sell it to get cash.  We did eat one chicken, I don’t know which one.  Our hostess did not charge us for it, though it was not included in the price of the meal.

 In Tutupalli, as everywhere else in Ecuador, we drank highly sweetened herb tea, cold or hot. In Tutupalli it was special.  Mostly we had Agua de la Reina Sofia, pure lemon grass and delicious.  After we admired the archeological site-a carved rock—in someone’s back yard—we were shown a begonia bush as big as a sofa, and later drank intense red tea made from the flowers.

          We stayed in an adobe house, quite warm; the bathroom (Running water! Toilet paper!) was reached by walking the length of the covered wooden veranda, then crossing the muddy yard on a twelve foot log plank and stepping stones.  There were electric lights on the square and in our building, as well as a jury rigged system in the clinic that could be turned on and off by pushing the plunger of a syringe in and out.  There was no TV, no radio, though the boy upstairs had at least one CD he liked a lot. At night we sat in the veranda and watched a group of six adolescents play volley ball for hours, no stopping for the rain. Like in Camelot, it apparently rained mostly at night there.

          The clinic was staffed by a health worker with limited training.  She offered condoms and a few medicines.  She was the only overweight person we saw in Tutupalli, as she spent most days indoors.  Working in the clinic tarnished the luster of Tutupalli a bit. The reality of subsistence farming was a harsh.  Kids were short.  They looked great while they were breast-fed; once weaned, it was clear that there had been a shortage of food when they were toddlers.  The smiley six year old at the river was a the size of a western 4 year old, 40 inches, 40 pounds.  The ten year old looked like a precocious American six year old. Most of my contemporaries were illiterate.  No one of any age wore glasses, whether they could see or not.

          In two days we collectively saw three women over forty who might be pregnant.  Two were, and not happy about it.  One complained bitterly of the nine children she already had and could not feed.  To obtain birth control other than condoms required a minimum monthly eight hour trip.  One had to take a one and a half hour hike and two hour bus ride to the closest government health center where one could get a monthly injection or package of pills.  (That is, if the soon-to-graduate medical student doing his or her rural practice was there, which was unreliably infrequent.)  That would be a monthly eight hour trip.  Alternatively, the two day round trip to Loja for a fifty dollar government subsidized IUD was a better deal, but fifty dollars was a months’ income for a family. In Ecuador, there are many IUFD's; (Intra-Uterine Fetal Demise); a dangerous alternative.

          Despite these limitations, Tutupalli was a wonderful place. Tranquil. Pleasant.  An organized community.  The people carried themselves with quiet self respect.  I was sad to leave.

          The day we left, we planned to ride mules up the hill so as not to waste time on our way to the next clinic.  I had forgotten that the first section of the path was a slick steep downhill stretch no more than forty degrees from perpendicular.  My mule had a wooden rack on its back that was normally used for tying on cargo, covered with a folded blanket. It had a two inch rim front and back and two shallow stirrups dangling from the rack by rope. My mule was led by an eleven year old girl I had treated the day before.  As she pulled its reins, the mule descended sharply.  I was trying to get my foot back in the stirrup, and the next thing I knew I fell over the top of the mule down the hill onto my head.  I must have missed the basketball sized rocks that studded the path, for I was not knocked out. After sitting and cursing in disbelief—luckily no one spoke English, so they could not understand the words at least of the doctor from the day before—I was able to walk up the hill to the clinic holding my scalp together.  I lost my really great hat.  I understand protected my scalp from the mud and mule droppings, but between the blood and the mud was deemed unsalvageable. 

As I mentioned previously, the dentist had a two small leftover vials of lidocaine and three sutures, so it was my lucky day.  I had a beautiful seven inch laceration down to the skull, which started in the middle of my forehead, turned  sharply two inches into the scalp to end about an inch above my right ear.  Sadly, there was no razor, so my scalp could not be shaved. (What a pity.)  While one of my colleagues sewed me up, the men from town got busy constructing a stretcher from four handhewn hardwood boards fastened together with nylon rope.  In the mean time, the clinic was called and an ambulance was dispatched to the end of the road.  (Had I mentioned that there was a telephone in Tutupalli?  One could even call the US from there.  There was not much privacy though; one sat at a desk yelling into the staticky phone while anyone who was interested—and many were—crowded around the large open window to listen in.)

          When the suturing and stretcher were finished, I was carried up the hill by eight local men, trading off, four at a time.  They made it in an hour, the same path it had taken me almost two hours to descend. They took off my rubber boots too lighten the load, but I doubt it helped much.  They made jokes as they jogged down the first steep slope and across the river, but on the way up had to save their breath.  I had two sand bags: literally, two garbage bags full of sand from the beach- the beautiful beach we swam at the day before.  At the top of the trail, I was transferred to a rudimentary ambulance. I kept my two sand bags, as they were the only ones available.  They leaked a little. The townspeople felt sorry for me when the sand got in my face.  But by that time, I was worried that might be the least of my problems.  I had a huge hematoma on the side of my head and a very sore neck.  Who knows what that meant. 

          Thus began the six hour ambulance trip: lying on my back clutching two leaking sandbags as we jolted over pot holed, unpaved roads, blood stiffening my hair and soaking my back, to Loja, sight of the nearest CT scanner.  Do I sound like I was feeling a bit sorry for myself?  I was worried.  Though I had walked, I was worried I had broken my neck.  My neck hurt more than my head the entire time. It still does.  From eight in the morning to 5:30 was a fairly long time to contemplate a possibly broken neck.  The funny part was, as the road gradually improved, becoming banked and graveled and ultimately paved, I heard my fall and injury being announced on the local news. I was famous. As they say, no good deed goes unpunished.

          I was not punished too harshly, though.  No broken neck, no subdural hematoma.  The CT Scans were cheap: only sixty dollars each, though my local radiologist made fun of the thickness of the slices.  I spent a few days in Loja, as my bus seatmate had suggested on the way down.  Actually, I spent most of the time in a small convent being cared for by a wonderful and very competent nun who was about to finish her nursing training.  I did get to see a bit of that pleasant city.  I was the subject of a sermon on sacrifice and suffering as a prerequisite for salvation.  I made it home in one piece, and recuperated. I now have only a large numb patch on my scalp, a red scar, and a stiff neck.  And a memory of beautiful Tutupalli.  I would like to return one day, before the road is finished and the trees are cut down and they yank the boulders out of the streets around the square.  Before it loses its sweetness.

 Judith Margaret Weiss Rivera, MD
