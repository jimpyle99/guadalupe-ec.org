Anaesthesia - Description of April 2005

by Dr. Gillian Davies

 

Our vision is to provide a good medical service to the poor deprived people in this area with mainly outpatient medical consultations. In  November 2001, the facility was opened, which is a medical clinic, not a hospital or emergency department.

There is an operating theatre, associated rooms, recovery, extensive pharmacy, lab, (no blood transfusion facilities) and dental department.  Most of the equipment and drugs are donated. The staff are all volunteers from Europe and the US, spending several weeks or months working in the clinic dealing with a varied  scattered population of 30,000,  helped by 4 local girls (no medical training), and a permanent clinic nurse who organizes everything

 

Surgery

There have been surgeons (eye, general and ENT). Complete teams to include theatre technician, and/or scrub nurse, post op nurse, plus one for night duty, would be ideal for more efficient safe surgery.  The specialty is advertised in advance so that patients arrive on days allocated for screening and those seen during the preceding six months.  A fee of 10 to 20 $ is charged per operation. Teams need to bring most of their equipment and drugs including  all anaesthetics for the procedures they plan to do which must be communicated in advance so that maximum benefit accrues from the visit.

 

Facilities on site are:

  • Operating theatre

described “as oasis of clean modern facilities in a remote location surrounded by poverty”, is  a tiled spacious room without windows, artificial lighting, dehumidifier,  OR table, OR light,  suction, large heavy oxygen cylinders (borrowed)  tilting trolley,  ECG machine, defibrillator, diathermy, instrument trolley. Some basic surgical instruments are here but it is necessary to bring those required, including disposable gown packs, sterile drapes, gloves etc,

Scrub room next to the theatre and a preparation room behind, in which are 8 small sterilizers, sinks, store, where instruments are washed and sorted for operations.  

  • Anaesthetic Equipment

There is a donated Narkomed Compact North American Drager with ventilator (recently repaired), pressure cycled, soda lime, isoflurane and ethrane vapourisers, oxygen only used from cylinders, no nitrous oxide or compressed air. The machine is really just a source of oxygen, space labs automatic BP machine and ECG, Schuco-vac suction machine, and 1 battery operated Respironics 950 oximeter. There was a poor selection of OT tubes, double circuit, rebreathing bags, on site .such that these must be brought, including laryngeal masks , T piece, and IV  cannulas.

  • Recovery

is a large room currently  overcrowded  with 7 beds,  4 only would give proper patient access. It was not designed for patients  to stay overnight. There is currently no oxygen, no suction, no monitors or resuscitation equipment in the area.

  • Drugs

Volunteers must bring all drugs they intend to use, since those in the clinic have been left by others, the supply is not consistent, and may not be in date,  ketamine and some local anaesthetics are available,  resuscitation drugs are required. The last expatriate anaesthetist brought propofol, (and automatic syringe driver) rocuronium, fentanyl, pethidine, diclofenac, midazolam spinal marcaine, lignocaine, ephedrine, atropine and prostigmine for the general team. The clinic buys in  N/Saline only for IV use.  Halothane is available in Ecuador, and surprisingly servoflurane. It costs each patient an extra 20 $, for halothane  plus there are no gas extraction facilities, inadequate air circulation in theatre and no vapouriser conversion scales.

 

Cases done

  • Eyes

Ptygeriums, cataract extractions with IOL, using local drops and retrobulbar block given by the surgeon. No sedation was used or monitoring.

  • General surgery

Given the current facilities, only hernias  were done on fit adult patients, 4 procedures a day using spinal anaesthesia,  plain marcaine, (inguinal hernias)  and  GA, consisting of propofol drip, with laryngeal mask or endotracheal intubation,  using rocuronium and IPPV with oxygen (epigastric, umbilical and incisional  hernias)  None of the usual Developing World Techniques were used.

  • ENT

Mainly nose and ear procedures were done in adults, up to 5 per day, using local anaesthesia, valium and ketamine. The use of untrained personnel giving these drugs without monitoring or readily available resuscitation needs must be discouraged, A local anesthetist was employed  for GA lists bringing her own supplies which were thiopentone, vecuronium and halothane.

 

Post Operative Care

Facilities are adequate for cases done under LA to rest a few hours, but not for immediate GA recovery or 24 hour supervision, or inpatient recovery of 2-3 days duration.

Patients did, in fact, stay overnight after hernia surgery. A local nurse was employed  without special knowledge of recovery duties.  She gave antibiotics, analgesics, etc.  and recorded vital signs. Relatives also stayed to help  providing personal care and food.

It is not the intention of the clinic to provide surgery with extended  inpatient recovery, but to offer day case facilities. There is absolutely no home care, patients come long distances by bus, pick up, horse, canoe, foot, such that the surgery and patients selected must be appropriate for the prevailing conditions.  A local hotel provides simple accommodation only.

 

Conclusion

It is a caring environment, an oasis of medical care in the jungle,  for  the poor of the Ecuadorian Oriente, we should like to see the surgical facilities being developed and used further.  It is an ideal place for organizations such as Rotary, to send specialist teams to work in Plastic surgery, Orthopedics, Gynaecology and Urology in addition to those described,  Those interested please contact Padre Jorge  nigsch57@easynet.net.ec
