Medical Brigade in March 2004

The beauty of Ecuador and the Ecuadorian people is impressive. The team had an amazing and successful adventure. The medical mission team arrived in Quito Sunday the 14th day of March, with plans to overnight in Quito and travel by plane the next morning to Catamayo and Loja for a 5-hour bus ride to El Pangui, located in a remote area of the Amazon basin. These plans changed suddenly as we went to pick up our airline tickets and were asked if we could fly in an hour, as space was available. Tame airlines waved the fee for the transport of the medications (12 large duffle bags), saving us a sizable amount of money. The change in plans worked out great and we had a restful nights sleep in Loja before joining our two translators and Padre Jorge Nigsch at the bus station the next morning.
The plans changed again when we arrived in El Pangui. Time had been set aside for orientation and setting up the pharmacy. However, when we arrived at 3:30 PM, patients were lined up to be seen. We organized in half an hour and saw patients in the high school classrooms. The first two nights we slept on mats placed on the classroom floor. The mats were provided by the military and very comfortable. We arose early the next morning and visited an orchid farm and then worked for 16 hours seeing patients. We saw a wide variety of disease processes, including textbook cases of hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism that were at advanced stages. We also saw some very unusual cases, like the man who had been bitten by a poisonous snake several years ago and he had a large (golfball size) lesion on the top of his right hand proximal to the bite. We continued to see patients in El Pangui the following day, but were unable to see all the patients who wanted to be seen.
In the evening, we traveled to Los Encuentros to see patients for 2 days. Beena Kamath, a pediatrician from Denver, joined us in Los Encuentros. Because she has been working at the Guadalupe Clinic for the last 10 months, her input on local diseases and pediatric problems was invaluable. The Ecuadorian military worked with us to provide crowd control and take blood pressures and pulse rates. They were very supportive and helpful. After 2 days in Los Encentros, we traveled to Paquisha (a great place) to see patients for 2 days. At Paquisha, we did a home visit on a 72-year old man who was dying of cancer. He had a huge lymphatic tumor in his right axillary area. The tumor and arm were infected, requiring dressing changes every one to two hours. The dressings supplied by Heart-To-Heart were invaluable to his treatment. We were able to treat the pain and infection with the medicines supplied by Heart-To-Heart. By the time we left Paquisha, 4 days later, he was doing much better.
While we were in Paquisha, several of the US and Ecuadorian team members became ill. The medications supplied by Heart-to-Heart were very appreciated by all of us.
On Monday of our second week, we took a day trip to Chinapintza, a gold mining community. We saw a great deal of physical and emotional abuse in this area. Many of the patients were very sick and had multiple health problems. The day ended with a tour of two gold mines. It was an eye-opener to the difficulty of the work the men endure. Our final day of seeing patients was spent in El Dorado, a sugar cane region.
Thanks to all of you at Heart-to-Heart International, we were able to provide treatments and medications to over 1300 people in remote areas of the Amazon basin of Ecuador. There are not words that can fully express the thankfulness of the Ecuadorian people for the gift of medications you gave to them. Thank you for making this medical mission a very successful project. The people at Clinica de Guadalupe also expressed their appreciation for the generosity of you and your donors.
Respectfully submitted,

Lorna Schumann
