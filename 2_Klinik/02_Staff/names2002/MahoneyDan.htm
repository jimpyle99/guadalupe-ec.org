March 29/05

Report by Dr. Dan Mahoney MD, FRCS(C)

I recently spent 2 weeks on a surgical mission at Padre Jorges mission clinic in Guadalupe.

Our mission was an experiment of sorts, to determine if general anaesthesia, and general surgery were feasible and safe in this setting. I feel we demonstrated very clearly that it was.

The clinic itself, is an oasis of clean, modern facilities, competent for conducting anaesthesia and surgical procedures, and was quite impressive considering the remote location, and surrounding poverty.

Our team consisted of:

  • Dr. Susan Bryan, Anaethetist from Thunder Bay  

  • Her husband, Mr. Mike Bryan (who although not a medical professional capably performed all duties of anaesthetic assistant and circulating nurse par excellance).

  • Myself, a community general surgeon from Chatham, Ontario.

  • My 2 sons Conrad (age 16) and Zack (age 12)

Conrad competently performed the cleaning and sterilizing of all our surgical instruments. (averaging 3 - 4 cases per day). Zack washed and cleaned the OR between cases.

We saw over 70 surgical consults . These patients were pre-screened by Amanda the clinic nurse and volunteer physicians to maximise our potential surgeries. However there were many consults for vaginal prolapse, hysterectomy, and prostatic obstruction, conditions which I  was unable to  deal with. I recommend that any team coming, communicate clearly a complete list of the procedures they would be willing to perform, to prevent seeing consults outside of their specialty repertoire. Having said that, there were many other cases such as hernias which we turned away  due to operating  time constraints, and several consults performed just to give a second opinion on a complicated problem.

We performed over 30 surgical procedures, primarily hernia repairs, various lumps and bumps, 1 massive parotid tumor, and several traumatic finger amputations.

As of 3 weeks post our last procedure I am not aware of any serious postop problems such as wound infections. One patient required a foley catheter overnight, several transient postop fevers and constipation were the only problems.

 

We had initially booked 4 open cholecystectomies, but after the first 2 days we reconsidered and cancelled them due to a number of concerns as follows:

  • inadequate postop recovery room facilities and lack of 24 hr. nursing supervision.

  • Lack of a functioning cautery unit (initially).

  • My personal insecurity wrt sponge counts for intra abdominal work.

The cautery and the postop recovery organization have been largely corrected at this point, and as such I think that GB and abdominal cavity work would be feasible in the future.

For this however, there needs to be 24 hr postop nursing supervision, and a commitment to keep such patients for the 2-4 days required for inpatient recovery. Remember, homecare is non existent here. Furthermore, Padre Jorge and Amanda prefer the  focus to be on procedures suitable for outpatients (or at most, overnight stays).

The OR itself was a pleasant surprise in a clean well maintained environment, with a functional anaesthetic machine, OR table, suction, lights and oxygen. They were simple, basic, entirely serviceable, and in good working order. Further concerns re: anesthesia should be directed to Dr. Susan Bryan.

All sterilisation was performed by a 16 yr. old high school student who learned on arrival. He handwashed all dirty instruments, repackaged and  sterilised them.

My primary recommendation for this area would be to obtain a hotwater machine washer, and to clarify the drying cycle for the autoclaves. (we could not quite get our instrument trays and covers dry most days). This room would also benefit from improved ventilation and/or airconditioning.

My primary concerns overall, were:

  • Ensuring sterile technique to accomplish a negligible infection rate.

  • Ensuring proper sponge and instrument counts

These are 2  very basic issues, and  areas that I normally don’t even need to  think about, however they were the cause of my only anxiety throughout the entire mission.

My takehome lesson for future surgical teams would be to include an experienced OR nurse, and if possible a recovery room/or surgical ward nurse.

 

An OR technition who could supervise and set up the optimal organization for the sterilization room (on one initial trip only) would be a very beneficial investment for all future missions as well.

The recovery room while basic will be entirely reasonable with several minor structural changes, including privacy curtains, widening the entrance doorway, and availability of suction and oxygen. I believe these are being implemented as I write.

In conclusion, I would say that both general anaesthesia, and general surgery, have been demonstrated to be  reasonable and safe at this clinic. I would expect the same to hold for both urology and gynecology.

The possibility of doing more major procedures at the regional hospital in Zamorra (1 hours drive away) also looks feasible to me. Although we did not do this, we did tour the OR’s, and this facility could provide the more extended inpatient recovery facilities that the clinic prefers not to develop.

In addition, we thoroughly enjoyed ourselves during our stay.

The clinic is staffed entirely by volunteers, and we met many dedicated doctors and dentists from Germany, The Netherlands and  England. Accommodation was quite comfortable, the sisters very gracious and hospitable hosts, and the meals delicious and enjoyable, with an eclectic international flavor.

In addition we got together for several  parties, and a concert arranged for us by Padre Jorge Nigsch. Again a refreshing and thoroughly enjoyable change of pace for all of us.

I hope these thoughts are of some benefit, and I would be happy to discuss further any enquiries.
