Primary care health care workers including physicians and advanced nurse practitioners, and dentists, are needed to help see the indigenous population that is served by this modern clinic in the Ecuadorian tropical highland along the Peruvian border. There is also a need for ophthalmologists, otolaryngologists, plastic surgeons, and general surgeons in the near future when the operating room is fully functional. The clinic is situated at a Catholic mission in the village of Guadalupe along the main trade route into the Amazon. The nearest hospital is several hours away. The clinic has examining rooms, an operating room, and dental rooms with dental x-ray. There is a well stocked pharmacy. There is a rudimentary laboratory. Some access to commercial laboratory and radiology is available. Volunteers can come for as little as two weeks or for several months. Travel is not reimbursed but room and board is covered with comfortable accommodations at the mission. A knowledge of Spanish is helpful however a bilingual nurse is present. All faiths are welcome, but respect for religion is essential.

I wished to give an update on the progress of this three year old facility. I have had the opportunity to volunteer on three occasions at this modern medical clinic. It is located on the grounds of a Catholic mission in the Ecuadorean Amazon, the Oriente, along the Peruvian border. There are examining rooms, dental suites, an operating theatre, a pharmacy, and a laboratory. Currently attending is a pediatrician from the United States who has been practicing general medicine for nine months, and will do so for another three. In addition there have been, and will continue to be, a steady stream of physicians coming for shorter stays, including internists, general practitioners, pediatricians, ophthalmologists and an otolaryngologist. There have also been physician assistants and advanced nurse practitioners. They have begun outreach programs and periodic mobile health fairs to even more remote locations. The facility serves an immediate population consisting of about three thousand persons in seven villages, but has remarkably been drawing patients from the entire provence of thirty thousand. Between the dentists, primary care physicians and surgeons, the average patient burden is about fifty visits each day and up to a staggering and overwhelming two hundred when surgury is performed. There is an opportunity for a volunteer primary care physician to work for several months to a year in addition to those who are able to come for a briefer sojourn. This might enable a certain amount of continuity of care supplemented by the efforts of other shorter term health care workers. Openings start late this coming summer into next year for internists, pediatricians, family and general practitioners, ophthalmologists, otolaryngologists and dentists. Room and board is free but travel is not reimbursed. Although a knowledge of Spanish is helpful there is a bilingual nurse on premise. Based at a Catholic mission, all faiths are welcome, but a respect for religion is important. ....... We were at the Mission Clinic for one week in December 2001 when it opened. There is a local physician Dr. Jaime Ortiz who has been hired to attend once per week. There have been a stream of foreign dentists and last week 4 physicians from a city 3 hours away. The clinic has examining rooms, 2 dental rooms complete with dental x-ray, a surgical suite including a scrub room and recovery, and a pharmacy. The surgical area is not yet functional. Although the pharmacy has limitations we were able to treat most of what we saw. The clinic may soon have a basic laboratory. There is x-ray, laboratory, and surgical services in a city (Loja) 2 hours away for the few who can afford it and when the road is passable.
The people were indiginous subsistance farmers with a few shopkeepers and miners. The gold mines are about one hour away. The oil companies are not in this particular region. There is malaria in the general area but little in the immediate vacinity. Similarly we did not see dengue or yellow fever. Mosquitos were present but not in great numbers. The water is not clean and that coupled with often the people sharing accomodations with their animals, giardia and worms were endemic. We saw some acute diarrhea and perhaps one person with typhoid fever. Many people had back pain (called kidney problems!) as well as depression, alcoholism, hypertension, and children with chronic ruptured tympanic membranes. We also saw all manner of other medical illness from dysfunctional vaginal bleeding to rheumatoid arthritis to parkinsonism to kidney stones. Some access to birth control is available in the city. Remarkably some people of greater means came from the city to this jungle clinic for second opinion, records in hand. Surgical supplies were present for suturing, draining, packing and casting, but analgesics and local anesthetics were deficient. Hopefully this has been rectified. The examining rooms are equipt but you may wish to bring a portable ophthalmoscope/otoscope as a backup as well as a glucometer, gloves, gel, and hemoccult cards.
The parish is clean and accommodations quite comfortable with our own rooms and hot shower. The Nuns supplied us with preboiled water to drink and hearty meals safe to eat. We even felt comfortable eating the salads served to us by them, usually not a good idea in this setting. The Padre is from Austria. He has done remarkable things for his people in a place where the church does the social service tasks that are usually the purvue of the government. The government gives vaccinations sporatically and thus far this is not being performed by the clinic but perhaps in the future. The village is in the high tropical jungle along the main trade route to the amazon in Ecuador. It is poor but beautiful. I suggest that you be up to date for dT, polio, hepatitis a+b, and obtain yellow fever vaccine. You will need malaria prophyllaxis. I suggest a book Handbook of Medicine in Developing Countries by Catherine Wolf M.D. and Dennis Palmer D.O. Although mainly geared to the hospital it has usefull information in table format.
The weather is most pleasant. It is about 75*F during the day, sometimes warmer, and 65*F at night, again sometimes warmer. It rains every day. I brought scrubs and a laboratory coat to work in and was most comfortable and felt adequately attired. One has to be wary of crime in Quito or Guayaquil but I felt perfectly safe in Guadalupe, especially as a guest of the parish mission. The currency is the US dollar. It goes far in Ecuador. There is little to spend it on in the village. We did not have a chance to be tourists but the beauty of the surroundings and the reward of helping others who appreciated the effort more than made up for it. They could use an operating microscope. If you know of any institution who might be able to donate one that would be terrific. That way ENT and basic Ophthalmology could be performed. Many children have ruptured TM's and many adults have cataracts and pterygeums.
 

The patients love lab tests! There seems to be no discernment in ordering tests to find out specific information; there is just a big random ordering of whatever tests for whatever reasons, without taking the time to think things through. Patients love to screen themselves for different diseases, and can go to a privately owned lab, where the lab owner is only too happy to mark up a lab sheet charging the patient for a CBC, lipid panel, BUN, Cr, SGOT, SGPT, Creactive protein, ASTO, Rhematoid factor. The patient will then bring these tests to you, having made their own diagnosis! (1)    Incorrect use of medicine:  In any store or local pharmacy, a patient can walk in and buy medicine without a doctor’s prescription.  I have seen women walk into a pharmacy and purchase a combination pill with Fluphenazine and Nortriptyline for an infant’s colic!  (Regulations are starting to pop up to control this more and more, but as of yet, there has not been much control over this unofficial use of medication.)  Thus a patient will often self-treat with medication or natural remedies before arriving to the Clinic.  Most commonly, people will buy one Ampicillin (Ampibex) tablet for a cold or skin infection, or a Naprosyn (Apronax) for pain or infection.  (2)    Desire for Injections:  Many patients desire injections.  There is a conception that injections work faster and stronger than other medicines, even though they are more expensive.  In addition, patients think that one injection will cure an illness.  For example, many use one injection of Gentamicin to cure a common cold.  Many want “sueros,” or IV injections of vitamins or other medicines to cure illness, and some even know how to put in their own IVs at home to give intravenous medications.  Another example is IM Diclofenac for musculoskeletal pain.  The most dangerous example are the patients who desire IV CALCIUM for their bone pains!  Patients do occasionally share needles with injections.  The perception exists that a quick shot of medicine is all they need to cure most illness.  In my time here, I have rarely given injections as to not feed into this dependence on injections and instead have tried to educate on the risks of injections, such as higher cost, risk of infection, etc.(3)    Misunderstanding of Chronic Treatments:  Because of this desire for a “quick fix,” an injection that will quickly cure their illness, there needs to be more education regarding chronic illness, for example, high blood pressure and diabetes, illnesses that are monitored on a chronic basis and not treatable with a quick ten day course of medicine.  Patients need to understand clearly that they have a chronic illness, and when you want to see them back. (4)    Vitamins:  The patients are very concerned regarding vitamin deficiencies, although I have rarely seen a clearcut case of vitamin deficiency.  Instead of dispensing a lot of vitamins, which are expensive, I have tried to do more education of the balanced diet, and have only given vitamins to pregnant women or to children with poor growth.  I have tried to avoid reinforcing this worry about vitamin deficiencies by giving vitamins, except in clearcut cases where I have seen actually vitamin deficiencies exist.  Many local doctors use Vitamin B shots (in the forms of Rojamin, Neurogime, Neurobion, Beco 5) to cure a variety of illness to miscarriage, musculoskeletal pain, any medical complaint.  Unfortunately, our Clinic does carry the Vitamin B shots, which does send a mixed message to patients, even though I have NEVER prescribed these during my time here.  It would be a goal to STOP CARRYING VITAMIN B PRODUCTS IN OUR PHARMACY.   Please continue to argue against Vitamin B in the pharmacy. (5)    Stopping Treatments Early:  Because of their desire for a quick fix, the patients are unsatisfied if the medicines do not work right away.  They are impatient to take a full course of medicine for 7 to 10 days, and will often take the treatment for a day or so before visiting another doctor, who will most likely change your treatment.  For this reason, I try to reinforce the duration of treatment, and why it is important to complete the treatment, and if the treatment does not work well, make sure you tell them to come back.  If they know that you want to see them again, they will return instead of going to someone else.  Also, I try to give a clear idea of how many days they will need to get better, and return precautions, symptoms or scenarios in which I will worry that the treatment is not working well.  For example,  “Your child has a cold.  His body will fight this over the course of 5-7 days.  If he has fever that lasts more than 3 days, vomiting with blood, pus draining from his ear, is not drinking enough to produce one urine every eight hours, or other worries, please come back right away.”  (6)    The patients always want some sort of medicine:  This can be a challenge, because the patients always feel that they must leave with SOMETHING.  So, for example, there are times when I do give medicine, if just to keep them from going to another doctor who may give them something completely useless, such as Vitamin B shots.  If my giving a child with a cold a cough syrup prevents them from going to another doctor for a shot of penicillin, I err on the side of the cough syrup.  The PLACEBO EFFECT is huge, otherwise, I doubt there would be much use for vitamin shots.  (7)    The patients like polypharmacy:  Sometimes it is unavoidable…. You have to give one person four or five medicines, but I try to stick to a fewer number of medicines to avoid confusion.(8)    The patients share medicines:   If one person uses a medicine that helps them, they will share it with their neighbor or husband whether or not they have discussed it with a doctor first.  Just beware that this occurs.
