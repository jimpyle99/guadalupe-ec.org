Clínica Misional "Nuestra Señora de Guadalupe"  -  A brief history:

  

Zamora-Chinchipe Province is home to about 90,000 individuals and abuts other Andean provinces of similar size. Most people in this agrarian region are indigenous, being Shuar, Saraguro or mixed heritage Indian/Spanish. The majority of the citizens live in rural villages and some larger towns which serve as trade, political, and religious centers. The vast majority of the people are subsistence farmers, miners, and small shopkeepers.

 

The government has placed poorly supplied and staffed health centers in most of the larger villages. They perform vaccination drives, and provide family planning services, and some child and maternal care. There are some private physicians for those who can afford them. A few regional hospitals are present.  Some have out patient services. The quality of care is variable and in general not the same standard available in the large urban centers along the coast and in the high Andean plateau in the north of the country. The result is that most of the people do not have consistent access to quality medical providers.

 

Padre Jorge Nigsch is the parish priest at an important Catholic mission along the main trade route into the Ecuadorian Andean cloud forest zone, known by them as either the Oriente or Amazonian part of the country. This mission was already a center of religious activity and teaching for the province when he opened a medical facility in October of 2001. The building eventually, over time, was to house examining rooms, dental suites, an operating theater, a pharmacy, and a laboratory. The first foreign physician volunteer arrived in December 2001. He and his accompanying nurse practitioner proceeded to set up the examining rooms, sort through stacked boxes of donated (and often non-functional or inappropriate) equipment and supplies, and proceeded to see patients. They set up charts, and reviewed the pharmacy inventory. Many drugs were from Europe from a mass donation, often not useable in this setting. The operating room had a table and old anesthesia machines and monitors. There were no other volunteers scheduled.

 

Subsequently several tasks needed to be accomplished to assist the Clinica Misional to succeed. The first project was to recruit more medical volunteers, initially for primary care then surgical and subspecialty care. This was done by listing the venue on a variety of web sites, sending faxes and letters to organizations, and talking with colleagues. Padre was encouraged to establish an “experience” section on the mission clinic web site as well as standards to be followed when soliciting personnel. Success has been measurable in that from January 2002 through this time there have been only two months without a primary care physician, and general surgeons, ophthalmologists, otolaryngologists, gynecologists, anesthesiologists, and gastroenterologists have visited this venue. The next task was to locate an operating microscope, have the company discount it, and have a donor subsidize its purchase and transport to the facility. This has allowed a variety of surgical procedures to be performed including cataract implants and tympanic membrane reconstructions. Optometric equipment was donated, and the Lions International has forwarded thousands of recycled eyeglasses to be distributed. To supplement the pharmacy pharmaceutical company representatives donated large amounts of medication that supplemented purchased drugs. In turn these were mailed to Ecuador. In the first four years this surpassed the donations from Austria and Germany that had been the mainstay of supply until finally the clinic was certified to receive discounted medicines from international relief organizations. A donated new electrocardiograph was brought down. About two years later a laboratory was set up, enabled by the generosity of many individuals. This included centrifuges, semi-automated desk top chemistry analyzer, a QBC blood count machine, two laboratory microscopes, a lipid meter, and an assortment of supplies and reagents. A laboratory technician was recruited to further establish procedures and explore avenues of restocking material. Periodically solicited donations of replacement equipment have been required to keep the machines in working order. Although not as complete as one would wish, already some laboratory services surpass that available in the regional capital Zamora one hour away. Dental x-rays had been available, but all other radiographic services required a trip to Zamora, and were often unaffordable. Initially they utilized an obstetrical doppler, but in 2006 a complete portable ultrasound unit was sent. This machine is capable of both vascular and visceral studies. Since having been set up in October, it has been used on an almost daily basis. General radiology remains beyond the scope of this rural facility.

 

In the first four years of existence through 2005 this remote health center had more than 25,000 patient visits. In addition to surgical specialty care, out reach is now being done to even more distant, isolated, or underserved locations via mobile health fairs. In October 2006 three American physicians and a German dentist, accompanied by an Ecuadorian translator, an American nurse, and a Colombian medical assistant, participated in such an event for one week. They visited a gold mining town high in the Andes and a remote village reachable only by mule deep in a valley. They saw 465 medical patients in addition to giving adjuvant dental services, including extraction of 120 severely rotted teeth. In November 2006 a group of American nurse practitioners and Emergency Physicians, with Ecuadorian civilian and military support, are providing gynecological services to remote villages only reachable by motorized canoe. Each of these mobile health clinics is within one to two days travel from the main facility in Guadalupe, allowing continuity of care. In addition the villagers can be assured that another such mission will arrive every three to four months, organized by the Clinica Misional.

On separate occasions a Yale medical student and a Yale graduate level nursing student have worked at this venue. German medical students have also visited, and German dentals students, with supervision, have been integrated into providing care on a regular basis.

 

Last year a project was begun to fundraise for this venue. In order to do this a tax except 501 C foundation was established, “The Friends of the Mission Clinic of Our Lady of Guadalupe, Inc.” There have been mailings, and talks in front of civic organizations.

 

At the 2004 American College of Physicians annual meeting panel discussion on international volunteerism, this medical facility was highlighted.

 

As much as any volunteer has given to this endeavor, they have gained more. Each individual has the knowledge that his or her efforts have impacted favorably upon the quality of life of the citizenry in an underserved community in the developing world. Each professional is an ambassador for humanity.

 

Those who choose to support this venture can be assured that their efforts and contributions will bear fruit in tangible ways, witnessed daily by the indigenous peoples Southern Ecuador.  Even small contributions can have a tremendous positive impact in this setting.
