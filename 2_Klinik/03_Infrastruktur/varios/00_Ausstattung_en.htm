Building and Equipment

 
Primary care health care workers including physicians and advanced nurse practitioners, and dentists, are needed to help see the indigenous population that is served by this modern clinic in the Ecuadorian tropical highland along the Peruvian border. There is also a need for ophthalmologists, otolaryngologists, plastic surgeons, and general surgeons in the near future when the operating room is fully functional. The clinic is situated at a Catholic mission in the village of Guadalupe along the main trade route into the Amazon. The nearest hospital is several hours away. The clinic has examining rooms, an operating room, and dental rooms with dental x-ray. There is a well stocked pharmacy. There is a rudimentary laboratory. Some access to commercial laboratory and radiology is available. A local physician works part time as well as occasional foreign personnel. Volunteers can come for as little as one week or for several months. Travel is not reimbursed but room and board is covered with comfortable accommodations at the mission. A knowledge of Spanish and respect for religion is essential.

Dr. Serle Epstein describes the clinic in a letter to a possible volunter doctor  this way in January 2003:
I am an Internist and have had the opportunity to volunteer in Guadalupe Ecuador on two occasions. The clinic is remarkable with examining rooms, a fairly complete pharmacy, and a broad range of medical and surgical supplies as well as fully equipped dental rooms with dental x-ray. The EKG machine could be better... There is a rudimentary laboratory but no general radiology, although both are available in the community. There is a surgical suite with scrub and recovery area. As noted by P. Jorge the various anesthesia and monitoring equipment has yet to be tested. A plastic surgery group is considering visiting to set up the suite and return to perform cleft palate and other procedures, but a firm date is not set. An operating microscope is on route, and several German ophthalmologists are planning to come within the year to perform cataract and pterygeum procedures. If you were to volunteer at the clinic you could perform a great service the first few days helping to organize the operating room, testing what is available and guiding P. Jorge and the full time nurse Amanda Anderson in obtaining necessary supplies and equipment and instruments in order to make the facility functional. Perhaps P. Jorge could arrange for a local anesthesiologist to overlap your visit to help in this task and, if feasible, help you in performing hernia or other procedures that could be done under local anesthesia with sedation. Your visit would be well publicized so that patients are lined up for you, perhaps prescreened by a general practitioner. You may have to initially use better surgical instruments brought by you, even though basics such as clamps, retractors, pickups, needle holders, and various scissors are there, of varying quality. Two autoclaves are present and used by the dentists. Although there is a steady, but interrupted, stream of medical volunteers, should you wish it there would also be ample general medical care to be provided to the indigenous people of the region. However your main role would be to help set up the clinic for general surgery for the future, with any procedures you perform a bonus.....


Serle M. Epstein, M.D.
smepstein@aol.com
 

"...  a clinic in Ecuador based at a mission in the Oriente region along the Peruvian border. It serves a local indiginous population of 3,ooo but in turn draws from a wider provincial population of about 30,000. The clinic has examining rooms, two dental suites, an operating theater, and an opthalmology/optometry suite. A rather complete pharmacy is on premise. There is a laboratory currently performing cbc, fungal and gram stains, pregnancy tests, vaginal smears, stool parasites, and urinalyses. There is an arrangement for PAP smears to be read at a regional cancer hospital. Some commercial chemistry measurement is available at a laboratory about 2 hours bus ride away.
The patients suffer from a variety of illnesses but this includes diabetes and heart disease. It would be of great use to be able to assess lipid profiles in this setting. There are medications in stock to intervene and manage dyslipidemias...."
(Dr. Serle Epstein, November 2003)
