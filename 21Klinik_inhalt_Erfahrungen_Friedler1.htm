Experience
Experiencia

   October 2002

El Ecuador es un pueblo extraño:

-   vive tranquilo al pies de los volcanes

-   esta sentado pobremente en montañas de oro

-   y se alegna con musica triste

 Alexander von Humbold

 

 

 

 

It has been said: “The people of Ecuador are an extraordinary people.  They live tranquil at the foot of volcanoes.  They sit poorly with their problems upon mountains of gold and they are happiest with their sad songs”.

To all of you who know and to those of you who don’t, my wife Laurie and I have embarked on a medical mission in the town of Guadalupe along the Yacuambi River sitting at the Northern foothills of the Amazon Rainforest/Jungle.  We left for Ecuador on the 18th of October. We will be here, working until the 25th of this month, and are due to arrive back in the States on the 29th.

 

 

 

My good friend and physician, Serle Epstein introduced me to the person in charge of this program, a priest named Father Georg Nigsch, fondly referred to by the people as Padre Jorge (pronounced Hore-Hay),. seen here on left. What this man has done for the people who live here and in the surrounding towns is nothing shy of a miracle. In the middle of abject poverty this priest has created a virtual Shangri-La with a medical/dental clinic to boot.

 

 

 

 

After a two and a half hour ride to Newark, a five hour flight to  Bogota, a 1 hour layover, a 1 hour flight to Quito and an additional 1 hour flight to the Loja airport, vaguely seen in the adjacent photo, down in the left center, through the clouds, located in a valley between the mile high mountains ...the landing is spine tingling. 

 

From there, by 4 wheel drive vehicle, you drive 3 hours mostly through mountain passes  and dirt roads, many of whom have been washed out by landslides due to the intense rains that occur in this region.  The journey here, needless to say is taxing.  The last part of the journey involves crossing a 500 foot long suspension foot bridge over the Yacuambi river.

The bridge swings as you walk and it is said that one walks across this bridge praying that it’s not him/her who’s on it when the bridge finally collapses. Built in the sixties by the peace corps, the bridge never is inspected for safety.

 

They say charity begins at home.  That may be true - and one may question why we chose to do this instead of a relief program closer to home, but we have never seen anything at home that even comes close to what exists here.

Even the poorest of the poor back home live like kings in comparison to some of these people.  But what is more interesting is that there are distinct degrees of poverty.  As one travels deeper into surrounding villages you can observe people who live barefoot in wooden homes whose quality and cleanliness is worse than any doghouse (literally) we’ve seen back in the states. 

 

After Sunday mass was given, Padre Jorge took us to meet a very old woman in one of these villages whose health has been failing, preventing her from attending mass.  Physically the woman resembles Yoda the Jedi Master from the movie Star Wars both in stature as well as appearance, except that she is completely hunched over, walking with two sticks as canes.  She is called Mama Maria and has been  immortalized in a large mural painting which the people created behind the pulpit in the local church. She is seen here in the extreme middle left in the background hunched over, wearing a black dress and a white shawl.  She lives in a shack on the outskirts of town and her only possession is a chicken which she has tethered to a cooking pot full of chicken feed.  It is located in her “kitchen” and it is there to supply her with her only food source...eggs. 

 

When we visit she cries because she has nothing to give us. So she goes to a corner of the shack where she gets out four eggs that she’s hidden and insists that we take them, ashamed of her lack of resource.  Yesterday, Laurie and the Nuns paid a visit.  Laurie and Sister Flor washed Maria’s feet and hands as she cried for clean clothes.  Laurie bought her food. The only problem is that the rats get into the food because there is no safe place to stock it. So she gave her my poncho to rap it up with.       

What amazes us even more is that, in spite of the poverty, people give whatever money they can spare at Mass so the “poor” people in Africa and Asia might not starve.

Life here in the mission seems a lot like a sleepover camp. There’s no real hot water showers but rather an electrical shower head that takes the chill off the incoming water. Meals are a communal thing here. The Nuns prepare all the meals - lot’s of veggies and starch. Not much meat or chicken or fish as they are scarce here .

At mealtime, the day’s adventures, experiences and plans are discussed ... very family-like. Afterwards, everyone chips in and forms a line to do the dishes...yes, by hand...wash, rinse, drip, then towel dry, in cold water!

The weather here, oddly enough, is somewhat mild with occasional rain. Temperatures vary during the day between 75° and 90° degrees F and around 50°at night time This is Ecuador...on the Equator...but because of our altitude, ~3000ft., it’s cooler here and less humid than at sea level. This translates into FEW MOSQUITOES thank G-D.

 

 

 

Clinic Life

 

 

To my professional colleagues who read this, clinic life is most interesting.  No onlays, veneers or implants here.  Dentistry  is practiced the way it was in the fifties. Amalgams with onlayed cusps,  simple composites, and lots of extractions are the rule.  Occasional anterior root canals but nothing posterior is treated endodontically because there is no consistency in follow-up.  The dentists that come down are only here for a week, three at most, and the volume is tremendous. They all have dental problems.

 My very first patient was an officer who had a gun.  I made sure that I did a very good job on him. 

 

 

There is another dentist, Dr. Lydia Lindner, from Germany practicing here with me. Father Georg is from Austria, so there is a large input of dentists from Germany.  Many of the materials here are from German manufacturers and reading the labels is a real trip.  Lydia and I share a single slow speed handpiece between both operatories.  Most instruments are sterilized in modern autoclaves between patients but not this lowspeed.  Surface disinfection only, otherwise nothing would get done. 

 

 

There is no high volume suction either so there’s a lot of improvisation going on.  Don’t get me wrong, there is a lot here in terms of materials but it’s all donated and so it’s not like you have a standard armamentarium like you are used to in your cozy little office back home.  We do our best with what’s given.   Strangely enough, I find many rudimentary skills I’ve learned since I was a child are put to use here. For example, darkroom technique: because there is no auto-processor, processing radiographs reminds me of when I was a kid and I had this little darkroom in the basement,  fingers swishing in the dark through photo chemicals.  It takes me a full half hour to process, individually, 7 films. 

 

No OSHA or HIPPA here.  There’s no privacy.  While you are working on patients there’s always a peanut gallery watching on through the open windows.  They love it!

 

 

Today I did something that I’ve spent my entire career trying to prevent, a procedure whose long-term consequences I’ve spent years correcting.  Today I extracted a permanent lower right first molar on an eight year old girl.  Back in the states this tooth could have been easily restored with a root canal and posterior restoration with cuspal protection...but not here.  It broke my heart but I’m sure there will be more to come before my stay is done. 

And, oh yes, did I mention the fees?  We’ll if you look closely at the ccompanying photo you will see the fee schedules for the Medical Services on the left and the Dental Services on the right.  Hopefully some of you speak Spanish.

 
For those of you who don’t the dental side reads: 
 
Extraction of One Tooth $2.00

Surgical Extraction

 $5.00
Filling with Amalgam $2.00
Filling with Plastic Resin $4.00 
Root Canal Treatment $10.00  (hey...big time!!!)
Cleaning of the Teeth (most haven’t had one their entire lives so you can imagine!...note: hand scaling only!!!) $2.00
Dentures (Flippers actually) $7.00
And For Each Tooth (on the flipper) $0.50 additional/tooth
Denture Repair $4.00

 

And, if you didn’t catch what’s going on the medical side,  Serle gets one dollar from each patient visit!  My first day here I performed around 25 procedures for a grand total of $50.50 for the day!!  Serle saw 30 patients for a grand total of $30.00 and he was in the clinic until 9:00 pm. 

After hours, when we’re not doing dentistry, we help Serle out on minor surgical procedures.  The other night, as we were about to retire, a villager came running across the bridge in a panic that a man back in town had cut his lower leg on a piece of glass and he was bleeding to death.  What happened next looked like the opening scene of the television series M.A.S.H. with everyone running to the helicopter to retrieve the wounded.  After he was brought over by stretcher we discovered that the source of his bleeding was simply from a 1/2 inch puncture wound in his right ankle.  Serle used dental anesthetic set-up for local, two small sutures, a standing ovation from 20 onlookers and a dog, and violá !!!


Serle Writes:     “Today I saw a 43 year old woman, six months pregnant, afraid of her alcoholic husband who beats her. The next patient was her husband... A 4 year old girl presents with a draining fistula from her tibia with osteomyelitis and possible osteosarcoma. She does not cry. Her father cannot afford to take her to an orthopedist. At an average of 20 years  complaint per person with vaginal discharge, all told, today I saw 500 years of vaginal discharge.

I believe that here the opportunity for family planning begins at menopause or, parenthetically, dependent on how high one is able to count. Families of 10+ children is not uncommon here. This is because of lack of resources, fear of child mortality and tremendous peer pressure to marry young, often to separate from parents, in order to leave home. Everyone is depressed. They seem to all have a very flat affect, almost zombie-like. After a while even I am depressed. Being poor, working in the fields, gold mines, and forest with little help for those unable to care for themselves (at any age) and with many mouths to feed with little food, does not lend to merriment.

Yet people are gracious, appreciative, and enduring with a strength and at times grace that we would be unable to muster in less harsh circumstance. But there is music, dance, and religious festivals. And all is not without humor: “Doctor, I have terrible headaches in the sun.” ...What make it better? ... “I wear my sombrero.” ... Then I suggest that you wear your sombrero... This patient waited 8 hours for her consultation! Many complain that their bones hurt when it is cold. However, we are in the Amazon!

Even though the medicine we practice may give us pause in terms of quality, the peasants have such tremendous problems, and so few places to turn for help, that our worst efforts become models of exceptional care. And the clinic that Padre has built could be placed in the middle of any city in the United States and not seem out of place. The people line up by the dozens to be seen. It is 10 o’clock at night and they are already showing up to be seen tomorrow. We just finished our day’s work. I will need to rest in order to have the energy and spirit to try to do, in my own way, G-D’s work”.

 

Finally, the clinic has fallen under some harsh criticism by the local medical community.  Padre brings in great medical and dental talent from societies with the most modern information and technology so the people flock to the clinic from over two hours away and are willing to sit all day if need be.  Even people who could be considered upper middle class, and could afford care, come from surrounding cities in an attempt to exploit the nearly free care because they don’t trust their own medical establishment.   The more successful the clinic is, the greater the economic threat to the medical community. Additionally, the pharmacy undercuts the price for medicinals also sold by the local physicians. So Georg needs to be careful not to “step on toes”.

 

 

 

Other Stuff

 

In addition to clinic life, which usually absorbs 12 hours of each day,  we have had journeys to various villages that surround the compound.  The other day I went to a Shuar Village named Kiim.  The path to these villages is not marked as one proceeds over rivers and down cliffs in the jungle.  It’s important to know when to take a right otherwise you would probably never get home!

 

 

The Shuar are the original head hunters; you know, shrunken heads et al!!  The are usually a more reserved people and Padre warned me not to take too many pictures (me?…yeah right).  I first met up with a man in his twenties.  When I mentioned to him in my broken spanish that I would like to take photos he was apparently reluctant but he finally decided to let me take one of him.  Now I have a digital camera which instantaneously shows you the result of the photo you’ve just shot.  When I showed him this we instantly became each other’s new best frind.  He took me to his house, he introduced me to all his family members, he showed me the place in the back where the extract the juice from the sugar cane plant to make moonshine.  He couldn’t get enough!! 

They offered me some the “brew” but travellers are advised to never consume any liquids that are not properly purified due to contamination with parasites.  I gracefully, but nervously, declined and hoped I had not insulted them, but rather, suggested we go back to the village center where Padre was serving the Mass.

 

 

 

 

At the end of the clinic week all of the volunteers were treated to a ceremonial thank you “recital” which included native dance and song.  Additionally I was invited to get up and play a few tunes.  At the end of the ceremony we were presented with certificates and handmade gifts as and expression of their deep gratitude.  We all knew that this ceremonial “thank you” came deeply from the heart and we found it truly touching.  After ward we went bak to the compund to decompress and reflect upon the week’s events.   

 

 

 

 

Padre Jorge usually has a special “dessert” at the end of the day that the Sister’s prepare for him, that only he has the priviledge of partaking in. A sort of private stash as it were.  It’s oat meal with warm milk and some malted chocolate from his homeland, Austria.  I felt honored when he invited me to partake in such a “sacred” ritual (although the expression on his face may make him appear less than eager in this photo…but I assure you he was actually looking forward to this ceremonial sharing of the stash).
 

 

 

 

Albert Schweitzer once wrote and I paraphrase:  “I don’t know what your destiny will be, but one thing I do know; the only ones among  you who will be truly happy are those of you who have sought and found how to serve”.  When you are in a place like this, you take pause for great personal reflection.  You understand, finally, the meaning of the word Doctor.  Our problems back home, in the greater scheme of things, are not really problems at all.  And we should thank G-D for those things that we believe we are entitled to have, when, in reality, they are real privileges that we should never take for granted.  This experience affirms our commitment for providing the very best of care back home.  It makes clear the preciousness of our families and friends.
