Orientation Guide to Clinica Misional Nuestra Señora de Guadalupe

 

Welcome to the Orientation Guide for our Clinic.  This information is based on my personal experiences accumulated through my year of working here in Guadalupe.  The Clinic has been an amazing place to work, where the patients are truly appreciative of your work and time, and come to call you “Doctors Who Cure.”  They leave their homes in the early morning hours to get in line before our doors open, and wait for hours for their turn.  It has been a privilege and responsibility to have this kind of trust from the people here.

 

However, working in the Clinic has also been very challenging.  The cultural differences, different concepts of medicine, lack of continuity of care, lack of adequate auxiliary staff, oftentimes very basic medicines and equipment, and language barrier can make treatment of the patients in this region very difficult.  In addition, coping with the slow rate at which change occurs, in trying to improve the standard of care of the clinic, can be frustrating at times.  As the patients come to trust you more and more and see that your type of medicine works, they will follow up, and then the rewards are fulfilling in working here!    The patients and their appreciation of what you do make this job worthwhile.

 

I hope that this guide is helpful to you as you begin your own experiences working here in this very special place.  I have been as honest and complete as I know how to be, with the bad and the good.  Overall, in my time here, there has been a wealth of improvement in our level of care, and the services that we have been able to provide.  Change does occur, if little by little (poco a poco….)  If you should have any questions, or anything warrants clarification, please drop a line to beena@guadalupe-ec.org

 

And if you want the real low down and dirty, definitely drop a line, and we can talk before you arrive.  Forgive my detail oriented musings….

 

 

The Patients

 

The patients range in age from newborn to old age.  Depending on the experiences and abilities of the doctors in the Clinic, you may be able to see the patients within your specialty.  For example, I am a pediatrician; however, during most of my tenure here, I have been the only doctor, and thus have been seeing all the patients, regardless of age. 

 

The patients range from very poor to the middle class, from living in the countryside as farmers to living in the city.  Most of the patients work in agriculture, using their machetes, planting, harvesting.  Patients may also have other jobs, including secretaries, teachers, bus drivers, construction workers, miners.  They have a range of economic wealth, some being unable to afford even the simplest treatments, to others, who travel from the bigger cities like Loja and will pay for many tests, consults and medicines. 

 

There are three main cultural groups:  the Saraguro, the mestizo, and the Shuar.  They tend to intermarry within their own indigenous groups, thus are relatively culturally homogenous.  The Saraguro men usually have long braided hair, with black short pants, and easy to distinguish black felt hats.  The women wear many layers of skirts, including a top pleated skirt, the anako, paired with a colorful blouse, and elaborate beaded jewelry.  The Shuar have a typical facial appearance with high cheekbones.  Both groups use traditional medicine, things such as “uña del gato”, or “sangre de dragon,” or “horchattas” or “aguas del monte” to cure illness before seeking modern medicine.

 

There are some important cultural distinctions that can make treating these patients more difficult.  First of all, the reliance upon traditional medicines and natural remedies cannot be discounted.  As a general rule, patients try natural remedies before entering your consultorio.  Secondly, their representation of illness is often more dramatic and the symptoms are different than what you may be used to.  Use all of your physical exam skills to determine if the symptoms are truly worrisome.  For example, I had a Shuar woman writhing with a complaint of all over body pain, with a pulse of 60.   Rely on your clinical judgment in these cases, as often, their history and exam do not mesh well.  The Shuar also have a reputation of putting blame on those that help them, looking for a reason that an illness was caused (there is much prejudice against them for this very reason).  I can give many experiences in which the Shuar have been extremely challenging patients for their reliance on traditional medicine, their lack of understanding of modern medicine, their refusal to go to the hospital in case of emergency.

           

Patient Knowledge

 

The patients’ lack of understanding regarding their own health problems can be frustrating.  They may have chronic illnesses, but have no understanding of what those are, and be unable to describe the condition.  In addition, they may have had surgeries, and be unable to tell you exactly why.  They may have been treated with medicine, and be unable to tell you what medicine, and why they were taking it.  

 

This is one of the greatest frustrations of working in the Clinic.  Doing a Past Medical History, Past Surgical History and Current Medication List can make you grit your teeth and want to scream.  I have had several patients tell me that they had “tumors” but were unable to tell me in which organ, and why the tumor was removed, or if it was cancerous!  Do not even think about asking for pathology reports….I even had one woman tell me that she kept her uterus in formaldehyde at home, and told me that she could bring the jar for me to examine.

 

Part of the problem, in my opinion, comes from a lack of education on the part of the patients, a misunderstanding of basic concepts of health, and a lack of patient education on the part of other doctors who have treated the patient and not given clear explanations for the illness nor the medications.

 

Thus, I have tried to do a lot of education regarding taking responsibility for one’s own health care, including bringing prior records, boxes or prescriptions of the medicine, or lab results, in addition to demanding from other doctors simple explanations for the illnesses that they have.   This is one of the biggest differences in health education that you can make during your time here.

 

Triage

 

Right now, the order in which you see patients is in the order in which they arrive, in fact, the patients will fight each other to cut in the line in order to be first, etc.  It is hard to place judgments on the order to see the patients when certain have traveled extremely far to arrive to see you.  There is no time for you to do the triage, nor has there been enough staff with the experience to triage well.  Thus, we tend to attend to the patients in the order in which they arrive.

 

Occasionally, the nurses will alert you to a particularly sick patient that needs attention earlier.  Many times, the patients will make themselves out to be sicker than they really are in order to be attended to earlier.  Thus, I let the nurses screen these patients so that I may be seeing the patients in the order in which they were assigned.  This is our rough system of triage, which is not always the greatest to pick up sick patients.  In addition, taking a patient out of order makes the other patients, who may have traveled further or waited longer, very angry.

 

Oftentimes, the patients with obvious emergencies, such as a deformed arm, a bleeding cut,  are attended to immediately, but others, with not so obvious sicknesses, are left waiting.  Thus, there are holes in this form of triage.  A cyanotic infant was not recognized.  Or I have been asked to see a child just because he was crying, without vital signs being taken.  There have been several times when asthmatics have sat in the hall for hours, waiting, when they could have been quickly evaluated, and steroids and albuterol nebulizers begun.  In addition, I have had children sit the hallway without anti-pyretics to bring down fever.  Little by little, we are improving on attending to the patients in a more orderly fashion.

 

Medicines

 

The Clinic has a very extensive pharmacy, with donated medicines from the United States and Austria.  There are antibiotics, analgesics, antiepileptics, antidiabetic medications, antihypertensive, and lipid lowering, among others.  In addition, we are able to buy medications from drug representatives here in Ecuador (yes, they still do exist here, and are just as squirrelly as in the U.S.!) when we are running low on something.  However, we rely heavily on donated medications.  Most of the medications that we can buy here, in my experience, do not have as good an effect, as strong an effect, or any effect at all.  (There is even one snake oils salesman that sells medicines out of his pick up truck… unfortunately, we buy a variety of concoctions from him, that probably do not do much for the people other than take their money!)

 

Challenges that I have faced regarding the prescribing of medicines includes:

 

(1)    Incorrect use of medicine:  In any store or local pharmacy, a patient can walk in and buy medicine without a doctor’s prescription.  I have seen women walk into a pharmacy and purchase a combination pill with Fluphenazine and Nortriptyline for an infant’s colic!  (Regulations are starting to pop up to control this more and more, but as of yet, there has not been much control over this unofficial use of medication.)  Thus a patient will often self-treat with medication or natural remedies before arriving to the Clinic.  Most commonly, people will buy one Ampicillin (Ampibex) tablet for a cold or skin infection, or a Naprosyn (Apronax) for pain or infection. 

 

(2)    Desire for Injections:  Many patients desire injections.  There is a conception that injections work faster and stronger than other medicines, even though they are more expensive.  In addition, patients think that one injection will cure an illness.  For example, many use one injection of Gentamicin to cure a common cold.  Many want “sueros,” or IV injections of vitamins or other medicines to cure illness, and some even know how to put in their own IVs at home to give intravenous medications.  Another example is IM Diclofenac for musculoskeletal pain.  The most dangerous example are the patients who desire IV CALCIUM for their bone pains!  Patients do occasionally share needles with injections.  The perception exists that a quick shot of medicine is all they need to cure most illness.  In my time here, I have rarely given injections as to not feed into this dependence on injections and instead have tried to educate on the risks of injections, such as higher cost, risk of infection, etc.  If the patient can swallow, I do not give injections.  I also try to educate the patients WHY injections are not needed, when the patient is capable of buying a tablet, that he can swallow, that costs less, and avoids risks on injections.

 

(3)    Misunderstanding of Chronic Treatments:  Because of this desire for a “quick fix,” an injection that will quickly cure their illness, the patients need to be more educated regarding chronic illness, for example, high blood pressure and diabetes, illnesses that are monitored on a chronic basis and not treatable with a quick ten day course of medicine.  Patients need to understand clearly that they have a chronic illness, and when you want to see them back.

 

(4)    Vitamins  (THIS ONE IS THE BIGGIE):  The patients are very concerned regarding vitamin deficiencies, although I have rarely seen a clearcut case of vitamin deficiency.  Instead of dispensing a lot of vitamins, which are expensive, I have tried to do more education of the balanced diet, and have only given vitamins to pregnant women, children with poor growth, or calcium to menopausal women.  I have tried to avoid reinforcing this worry about vitamin deficiencies by giving vitamins, except in clearcut cases where I have seen actually vitamin deficiencies exist.  Another problem is that the vitamins that we do carry in the pharmacy do not come in combinations that are useful for treating actual vitamin deficiencies.  For example, our vitamins for pregnant women do not have an adequate amount of folate.  The vitamins that we buy for children focus on different types of Vitamin B, rather than calcium or iron.  I have not been able to find a combination vitamin that has the amounts needed of recommended vitamins. Giving vitamins, without also a discussion of a balanced diet, in my opinion, is not providing quality care.  (It takes more time to explain a balanced diet, but the people have little understanding of this!!  Only giving vitamins reinforces the false belief that they have that many conditions can be cured with vitamins.)  Many local doctors use Vitamin B shots (in the forms of Rojamin, Neurogime, Neurobion, Beco 5) to cure a variety of illness to miscarriage, musculoskeletal pain, any medical complaint.  Unfortunately, our Clinic does carry the Vitamin B shots, which does send a mixed message to patients, even though I have NEVER prescribed these during my time here.  It would be a goal to STOP CARRYING VITAMIN B PRODUCTS IN OUR PHARMACY.   Please continue to argue against Vitamin B in the pharmacy.  We have had discussions regarding selling of Vitamin B, and currently, because our philosophy is to have a pharmacy that serves the other doctors in the region, and they prescribe Vitamin B, we continue to carry the Vitamin B. 

 

(5)    Stopping Treatments Early:  Because of their desire for a quick fix, the patients are unsatisfied if the medicines do not work right away.  They are impatient to take a full course of medicine for 7 to 10 days, and will often take the treatment for a day or so before visiting another doctor, who will most likely change your treatment.  For this reason, I try to reinforce the duration of treatment, and why it is important to complete the treatment, and if the treatment does not work well, make sure you tell them to come back.  If they know that you want to see them again, they will return instead of going to someone else.  Also, I try to give a clear idea of how many days they will need to get better, and return precautions, symptoms or scenarios in which I will worry that the treatment is not working well.  For example,  “Your child has a cold.  His body will fight this over the course of 5-7 days.  If he has fever that lasts more than 3 days, vomiting with blood, pus draining from his ear, is not drinking enough to produce one urine every eight hours, or other worries, please come back right away.” 

 

(6)    The patients always want some sort of medicine:  This can be a challenge, because the patients always feel that they must leave with SOMETHING.  So, for example, there are times when I do give medicine, if just to keep them from going to another doctor who may give them something completely useless, such as Vitamin B shots.  If my giving a child with a cold a cough syrup prevents them from going to another doctor for a shot of penicillin, I err on the side of the cough syrup.  The PLACEBO EFFECT is huge, otherwise, I doubt there would be much use for vitamin shots. 

 

(7)    The patients like polypharmacy:  Sometimes it is unavoidable…. You have to give one person four or five medicines, but I try to stick to a fewer number of medicines to avoid confusion.  If the patient comes in with a complaint for every organ system (and there are MANY of these patients!), I tell them clearly that I cannot address every problem today, and we will start with the most bothersome, so that they will take a few medicines correctly, instead of being confused with many medicines, that will then do nothing.

 

(8)    The patients share medicines:   If one person uses a medicine that helps them, they will share it with their neighbor or husband whether or not they have discussed it with a doctor first.  Just beware that this occurs.

 

(9)    The patients do not buy the course of medicine as you prescribed.  DO NOT ASSUME THAT PATIENTS BOUGHT EVERYTHING THAT YOU PRESCRIBED.  For the most part, Rita, the woman who works in the pharmacy, knows the difference between antibiotics and other medicines, and she will not sell a partial treatment for antibiotics.  I have also asked for some better communication when the patients cannot buy the medicine on the prescription exactly as written, so that I can indicate to her which medicines are more important to sell.  For example, an asthmatic patient was buying Singulair instead of her steroid inhaler.  Do not assume that the patients bought everything that you prescribed.

 

Because the supply of certain medicines may be limited, for example, in my time here, we have been lacking beta agonist inhalers, I have tried to save the medicine for the sicker patients that I have with asthma.  However, other doctors receive the list of our medications, and use our pharmacy as the source of their prescriptions, and often they do not use the medication correctly.  This can be extremely frustrating, as oftentimes, they are overtreating a patient, or using a medicine for a condition for which it does not serve…. This has often occurred with inhalers, antibiotics (specifically flouroquinolones and Augmentin), even blood pressure, cholesterol or diabetes medicines.  Even if you have a plan for how to allocate the medicine, you are not the only one using it.  Again, I could discuss the use of injections to cure everything.  They may give one dose of Ampicillin (Ampibex) to cure skin infections or the common cold.  I could discuss vitamin shots.  I could discuss the misuse of antibiotics. 

 

This can be frustrating, when the supply of medicine is limited, and it is not being used correctly.   Right now the philosophy of the pharmacy is that it is to serve other doctors of the region as well, even though the ways in which they prescribe medicine are different than ours (use of antibiotics for colds, vitamin shots, etc.).  Thus, right now, there are NO RULES controlling the sale of medicine by their prescriptions, so be aware that you may not have used the Augmentin supply, but little by little, it was being prescribed by someone else.  

 

Try to work with the dentists in the Clinic as well, so that they are not using your valuable medicine supply other than how you would want to use it!  For example, some of the dentists were using Augmentin as first line for dental abscesses, when cheaper and just as effective medicines are available.  Given Augmentin’s broad usage for other things, it would be nice to save it for more serious infections!

 

Antibiotic resistance is a bit of a problem, but I have not seen any studies on this here.  I still start with the common first line drugs (Amoxicillin for ear infections, Bactrim, Amoxicillin or Cephalexin for UTI in kids or Bactrim or Ciprofloxacin for UTI in adults, for example)  and give the patient guidelines for returning.

 

Writing Prescriptions

 

When you have decided what medicines you will use, you will write these down in a prescription.  The patients need everything written down very clearly, and will probably ask you for specific times and with what type of tea (“Aguita” or “Horchatta”) that they can take the medicine.  I write EVERY DETAIL as clearly as I can on the prescription, including the indication for the medicine.  Many patients are elderly, cannot see, cannot read, and are forgetful.  They will hopefully have someone to help them read your prescription later, and if you have written everything down completely, it will be a big help to this third party who did not hear your explanation.

 

For example:

 

Enalapril 5 mg #30

Toma una tableta antes del desayuno diario  (presión)

 

Aspirina 100 mg #30

Toma una tableta antes del desayuno diario (circulación)

 

Instead of putting down specific times, I tended to say “before breakfast” for one time per day medicines or “before breakfast and dinner” for twice a day medicines, just to make things easier. 

 

For patients with chronic illness that I would like to see again, I tend to do things on a month by month basis.  There is enough time for the medicine to take an effect, and the patient is not required to buy too much of the medicine all at once, as the economic limitations are important to remember.

 

Oftentimes, patients will ask you to write for medicines for patients that you have not seen.  I have made it my policy to only write prescriptions for patients that I have actually seen, except for over the counter medicines.  The pharmacy will often sell parasite treatments without a prescription.

 

In addition, oftentimes, the nurses will ask me to review the prescription of another doctor to substitute another medicine or change a prescription.  The policy of the Clinic has been to serve the other doctors, carrying medicines that normally we would not use, such as Vitamin B shots, and other medicines.  In my opinion, then, changing a prescription without the knowledge of that doctor is incorrect.  Thus, when asked to change a prescription, I ask the nurses to call the doctor who prescribed the medicine, and communicate the problem with the prescription.  I would not want someone to change my prescription without my knowledge, and I am trying to give that same courtesy.  Otherwise, I have offered to see the patient, but that necessitates him buying another consulta.  I set this policy because while I do not agree with certain treatments of the other doctors such as Vitamin B, etc., and when I have argued that the pharmacy should not carry these medicines, I was told that our pharmacy is here to serve the other doctors of the region as well.  If we really want the pharmacy to serve the doctors of the region, the staff in the pharmacy has to communicate with those doctors directly if they want to change the prescription. 

 

The Clinic Schedule

 

The Clinic opens at 8 AM and closes at 5 PM, on Mondays through Fridays.   Lunch at the Convent starts at noon, and it is best to plan your day accordingly to be down at lunch as punctually as possible, although it is understood that you may be late on occasion, when dealing with a tough patient, etc. 

 

Dress Code

 

Scrubs are the preferable dress for the doctors in the Clinic.  There is a small supply of scrubs that you can borrow during your time here, or you may want to bring your own that fit, and then leave them upon your departure to increase our supply!

 

Patient Numbers

 

A lot of things affect the number of patients who arrive each day.  In general, Mondays and Fridays tend to be busier than most other days.  If, for some reason, the buses are not running (for example, in my time, landslides and teachers’ strikes have kept the buses from running at times), the numbers are low.  In addition, around holidays, fewer patients arrive.  During times where there are dentists or eye doctors, expect more patients to arrive.  With the recent eye doctor and ENT visitors, we have had almost 200 patients waiting for us each morning. 

 

It is up to YOU to decide the number of patients that you are capable of seeing.  To give a reference, I see 25 to 30 patients as a limit.  The most important thing is to do a complete consult, making sure that you understand what the patient wants from you, and making sure that the patient understands your advice.  When I first arrived, I was seeing over 30 patients a day, making quickie diagnoses, and unable to spend quality time with the patients, and I HATED IT because I was not enjoying the job, and was working late every day.  The patients appreciate the time that you spend, as some other doctors do not even take the time to examine the patients!   Also make sure that you know your own limits, because a doctor who is burned out is not much help to anyone.

 

When we have large patient numbers, for example, almost 200, we allow the patients to buy ONE ficha, for whichever specialist they most need.  If fichas are available after everyone has had a chance to buy one, we may allow them to buy a second.  In this way, everyone can have a chance to be seen.

 

Protecting Yourself

 

Working in the Clinic is rewarding, but it can be tiring.  Seeing a large number of patients, and changing your mindset to think of each individual’s problem and diagnosis can be mentally exhausting.  Know your limits, and be prepared to defend them.  The people will take advantage of all that you give them, and still ask for more, so know when you need to say NO.

 

Sometimes you may feel as though you are on call all the time, as the patients know where to find you, in the Residence right behind the Clinic.  Most patients know to come Monday through Friday, during Clinic hours.  But at all hours of the night and weekend, you may receive a knock on the door, or your window, asking for a consult, or to open the Clinic to buy medicine.  It is at your discretion to see patients at the other times.  If you do not feel that it is an emergency, be firm in your decision to make the person wait until the Clinic is open.  If you open the Clinic to one person, then everyone expects the same!  Be careful of getting yourself in this practice.  Padre Jorge and Amanda have been supportive of my setting limits.  Protect your free time. 

 

Occasionally, we have received requests to visit patients in their homes.  In special cases, I have agreed to do this.  We charge $1 for the ficha, and the patient’s family can come to the Clinic to buy the medicine.

 

People tend to bother Amanda (who has the key to the Clinic), the nurse, more than the doctors, in order to persuade her to open the Clinic to buy medicine.  The patients may go to the doctors in the Subcentro for the consulta and come with a prescription in the middle of the night.  Her practice has been to open the Clinic if the patient has a doctor’s prescription. 

 

Things that we have done to protect the doctors a bit more are to say NO when the patient does not have an emergency, to teach the people that we do NOT open the clinic at their discretion.  Also, Amanda opens the pharmacy for half an hour after Sunday Mass to sell medicine.  The patients thus have ample opportunity to plan to buy their medicine when the Clinic is open.

 

Perception of Foreign Doctors

 

Some people will prefer your care to that of an Ecuadorian doctor for the sheer fact that you are a foreigner.  Other patients will think that because you do not use vitamin shots or natural medicine, you cannot help them.  Because of the doctor shopping that often occurs, you have one chance to make a difference to a patient.   If you really communicate well your treatments despite the language barrier, they will come back.  If you give them the opportunity to know that you want them to follow up to provide continuity of care, they will.   Plus, you have medicine on your side (that is why we are called the “doctors that cure”)!  Give them a time frame for when they will get better, and when to come back if they are not. 

 

Other Doctors

 

Across the Hanging Bridge that leads from the Mission to the town of Guadalupe, there is a Subcentro of health, staffed by an Ecuadorian doctor (Dr. Alan) and dentist (Dr. Alvaro) and several nurses.  The nurses here function independently, doing consultas and prescribing medicine.  During my time here, there have been mostly friendly relations between the staff at the Subcentro and our Clinic.  Their staff uses the medicines from our pharmacy, which offers cheaper prices, to prescribe for their patients.  Our pharmacy stocks medicines that they specifically request, although the doctors here in the Clinic do not use, such as a variety of Vitamin shots.  The patients pay $0.50 for the ficha (ticket) there. 

 

The people of the town of Guadalupe itself tend to go to the Subcentro first for health care, because of the longstanding relationship of the local doctor, Dr. Alan, with the people.  However, as I have described earlier, the patients tend to be distrustful of doctors and impatient if treatments do not work right away.  Thus, they tend to doctor shop between the Subcentro, our Clinic, in addition to other clinics in Zamora or Loja. 

 

I have tried to balance giving good health care, and not badmouthing the local doctors, which sometimes can be challenging.  Dr. Alan does a lot of work on weekends and at night, when patients would otherwise be banging on my door.  I try to reinforce the people’s faith in him, even if I do not always agree with the treatment.  Overall, he cares about the people here and is a good doctor.

 

However, I would be dishonest to say that overall, the health care here in Ecuador is good.  Patients are distrustful of doctors and for good reason.  Many own their own pharmacies, and thus may prescribe three forms of Vitamin B shots under different names, to treat a variety of illness.  I have had patients who were frankly mismanaged for a variety of things, from congestive heart failure (having IV fluids given) to breast cysts (removed and noted to be benign, with the 30 year old patient put on Tamoxifen and being put into menopause) to numerous other cases.  The cases will shock you! 

 

Lab Tests

 

The patients love lab tests!   There seems to be no discernment in ordering tests to find out specific information; there is just a big random ordering of whatever tests for whatever reasons, without taking the time to think things through.  Patients love to screen themselves for different diseases, and can go to a privately owned lab, where the lab owner is only too happy to mark up a lab sheet charging the patient for a CBC, lipid panel, BUN, Cr, SGOT, SGPT, Creactive protein, ASTO, Rhematoid factor.  The patient will then bring these tests to you, having made their own diagnosis!

 

The lab tests are misused in a variety of ways.   First of all, many patients obtain lab tests without talking to a doctor first to see what lab tests are needed.  Secondly, the labs may not be doing the tests in the correct manner.  For example, the laboratories are not doing clean catch urines, so be wary of contaminated urine specimens, for which the patients are actually treated with antibiotics for infection.  Thirdly, the labs are ordering tests without doing consultas, and thinking through specifically what lab tests the patient needs and why.  Fourthly, I have been unsure at times how much to rely on the validity of some of the test results.  Patients will usually come to the Clinic “en ayunas” or fasting, in preparation to order many lab tests. 

 

The labs here use ASTO (ASO), PCR (C reactive protein) and Rhematoid factor to make the diagnosis of acute rheumatic fever, or rheumatism, often without a doctors’ consult first.  The patients are told that they need 6 months of penicillin shots, if they have an elevated ASTO or Rheumatoid factor.

 

We have the capability to do some basic lab tests, including

 

Blood Glucose $1

Urine Pregnancy Test $2

Complete Urinalysis $1

Urinalysis with Leukocytes and Nitrites $0.50

ECG $0.50

CBC $4

Cholesterol/Glucose $4

Papanicolau and wet prep $2

Stool prep (Free)

Hemoccult (Free)

 

We have recently received a machine with the capability of performing a variety of lab tests, including HbA1C, SGPT, cholesterol, triglycerides, CRP, sodium, potassium, and others.  As of now, we have not used the machine secondary to the need for micropipettes to measure out a very small quantity of blood and reagent to perform the test.  When we are able to obtain these micropipettes, it will also be important to figure out how much reagent to mix up, how long the reagent will last, and whether or not the test will in the long run be cheaper to do in another laboratory.  (If we are to make money on the test, this will be important, because it involves having to continually buy reagent, etc.)  This will be up to the doctors who follow me to decide.  My other concern is that for fear of letting reagents expire, tests will be unnecessarily ordered, and at the expense of the patients who cannot afford them. 

 

Because of the wide range of economic abilities of the patients, not everyone can afford a lot of lab tests.  Talk with your patient first if you want to order exams.  In my experience, ordering a lot of exams will sometimes turn them off, and then you will have lost them as a patient.  They will nod their head in agreement, but will not come back.  Or they may come back, and hang their head and say that they could not afford all of the tests.  This is a big difference from my experience in the US, where I am used to ordering lots of lab tests to confirm a diagnosis.  That is NOT feasible here.  I really try to ask myself what knowing a certain lab result will change about my management, because each lab test is a bit of the patients’ precious money that we are spending.  Will I treat if the result is abnormal?  Just ordering tests TO KNOW does not help the patients, and they will not come back.  Oftentimes, I may try a treatment, and ask the patient to follow-up, with the intention of ordering a lab test at that time, if things are not improving.  I try to make the doctor-patient relationship a collaboration, so that the patients know to return and talk if things are not getting better. 

 

It is also important to have a bit of suspicion regarding the validity of the tests.  I have had patients bring results of sodium of 93.  When I have ordered Xrays, they are done from an incorrect angle, and like I mentioned above, often, urine exams are not midstream clean catch specimens.

 

Your Consultorio and Resources

 

There are two general medical examining rooms in the Clinic, two rooms for the dentists, an operating room, a nurses’ station, a room for storage, a room for sterilization, and a room with a bed and the crash cart.  There is a laboratory where the nurses do the blood and urine testing, and also a microscope to view wet preps and stool preps.  The main medical examining room has two examining tables, both with stirrups to do pelvic exams.  In addition, there is an adult regular blood pressure cuff, and an ophthalmoscope/otoscope unit attached the wall.  A private bathroom is attached. 

 

There is also a computer in the main consultorio, with the ability to tell you the list of medications from the pharmacy, and how much of each medication is available.  There is also the ability to find out what medicines a person has bought in the past.  The computer is very helpful to look up costs of medications, and how many tablets of each medication that we have, to help you in individualizing your treatment, plus knowing what resources are available.   The medication and patient list, are for the most part, up to date, but room for improvement still exists to make the computer work better for us by entering medications under each individual patient’s name, rather than medications for a whole family under one name.  Sometimes the medicines are not correctly recorded under anyone’s name, so this system is not always completely reliable.

 

We are currently increasing the medical library in the Clinic, and the main consultorio has a bookshelf with many resources that we have received from other doctors, including: 

 

  • Harrison’s Internal Medicine, 15th Edition

  • Saunders Manual of Medical Practice

  • Physician’s Desk Reference 1999

Patient Finances

 

The patients come from a variety of economic backgrounds.  Our Clinic has the cheapest medical care and medicine available to the people in the region.  Still, there are some patients who may have a hard time affording what we offer.

 

Every patient must come with $1 for the initial ficha, the number in the line to see you.  We do not change or lower this price for anyone.  We practice the philosophy that everyone must contribute something to keep the clinic going, for their dignity, and plus in order to value the service that we provide. 

 

I cannot stress enough the importance of communication with the patients regarding their funds.  Especially for chronic illness, or infection, when you want them to comply with a treatment of a certain duration, the cost of the medicine may be greater than what they can afford.  Occasionally, I have had to lower a treatment duration from 10 days to 7 days, or change to a less expensive medicine in order to accommodate what a patient can afford.  We do not give medicine away, so that the Clinic may be self-sufficient. 

 

Some patients will tell you they have no money, when in reality, they do have some resources to buy medicine.  The staff in the Clinic (Rita and Amanda) have knowledge of certain individuals, and their level of poverty, and will let you know if a patient cannot buy the medicine as you have prescribed.  For the extremely poor patients, who would not be able to afford the treatments, Amanda is doing a guinea pig/rabbit breeding project, breeding and then selling the animals to produce funds for the people who could not afford their treatments otherwise.  Unbeknownst to some patients, they receive a little financial boost thanks to this project.

 

Vital Signs and Medical Records

 

In my year here, we have begun to do complete sets of vital signs, and I hope this practice will be continued.  I have found every vital sign to be useful in thinking about each patient.  For example, the weights are helpful, as many patients feel they are without appetite, and you can use the weight trend to point out if they are losing or gaining.  Simple, I know, but the patients are unaware of their weights, as they have no way to weight themselves at home.   Right now, the nurses are only getting a measure of temperature on people who have a history of fever, although some people fall through the cracks, and never have the measurement done.  (I am unclear why we cannot do temperature measurements on all the patients.)

We have recently received a child’s blood pressure cuff, and I encourage doing complete vitals signs on the child as well, INCLUDING blood pressure, which is not something that we were able to do previously.

 

The nurses know to do a rectal temperature on an infant.  For respiratory rates in an infant, you must count for 30 seconds to account for periodic breathing.

 

Please continue my practice of plotting the children on growth charts.  Being a pediatrician, I have found the growth charts to rule out a variety of serious illnesses, in addition to vitamin deficiencies, in addition to allaying parents’ fears that their children are not growing well.   In general, the children of Ecuador tend to be shorter than those in the US, especially when they are of the indigenous Shuar or Saraguro cultures.   One thing that you can do is to measure both parents, and calculate a midparental height.  If only one parent accompanies the child, I measure that parent’s height and make a mark on the growth chart. 

 

Please see the section on Special Pediatric Considerations to have a more detailed description of working up growth problems in children.

 

Right now, our system of filing the charts can be a bit aggravating.  I have never been allowed to touch the filing, in order that it not be confused more than it already is.  We often lose charts, or they are in the incorrect location, and this can be frustrating when following a chronic patient, and for the amount of time lost on busy days spent waiting on a missing chart.  We have been brainstorming ways to improve the charting system, but so far have not implemented any changes, for a variety of complicating factors.  The patients have two first names and last names, and if they do not give all four names, we may not find the correct chart, start a new chart on the same patient, or lose their chart altogether.  Sometimes the patients may give a different name in order that you not find their chart, so that they can get another medicine!  In addition, the patients have a cedula number to identify them, but they do not always carry the card with the number, thus at times, even though it may be able to serve like a Social Security Number, the patient may not have committed the number to memory.  There have been times when charts have been lost, or exams that have been done, not documented.  I have done my best to document as much as possible, but sometimes, all the documentation is lost in our mountains of charts.

 

Right now, we are trying to keep all the notes from general medicine and any medical specialists together in one chart, with the exception of eye and dental charts being separate.  I have found this to be extremely helpful in treating patients, as often, they do not remember what medicine or diagnosis the specialist gave.  With the plan to have more specialists, I hope that this practice will be continued, as having five different charts on one person makes the filing difficult and does not allow all of the medical personnel to have the complete information on the patient, nor collaborate together.  The system is far from perfect, as oftentimes, new charts are started when a patient already has an existing chart, but little by little, it is improving.

 

Perception of Illness

 

Because of the language and cultural barrier, it is even more important to me to take the time with the patients so that they understand my treatments.  They will then be more likely to follow them.  For that reason, I try to be very complete in my explanations of the disease, whether it is curable or the symptoms can be expected to occur, when they should follow up, and exactly how they need to take the medicine.  The more that you can explain to the patients, the more likely they will succeed in following your treatment plan and following up.

 

It was clear to me when I first began to work here that the patients think of you as a failure if you do not cure their disease.  This is a concept that they have regarding illness, that everything can be cured.  The most common example is arthritis.  The people have never been told that arthritis does not have a cure, only medicine to treat and calm the pain.  When they understand that a medicine exists to help them, and they can return when they finish the medicine that you have given, they are more likely to come back, instead of thinking that you have failed.

 

Follow-Up and Feedback

 

This environment can be tough in encouraging follow-up for a multitude of reasons.  First of all, it may be costly and time consuming for a patient to follow-up.  If a patient has to travel for 4 or 6 hours to get here, they may not come.  This can be an issue, for example, to the dentists, when they put in a drain, and need the patient to follow up to have the drain removed.  If the patient starts to feel better after the initiation of treatment, they may not come back.  For me, an example would be a patient that I have done an incision and drainage of an abscess.   If I ask the patient to come back in several days, he may not, if the antibiotics are helping him to get better.   I have sutured many lacerations, only to find that the patients do not come back to have their sutures out.  This also occurs with chronic illnesses such as hypertension:  when the patient’s hypertensive headache is relieved by your medicine, he may not come back, if he does not understand that once he suspends the medicine, his headache will return.

 

Oftentimes, the feedback part of patient care is lacking.  Most of us in medicine like to see our patients after they have been helped by our treatments, but in the cases where our patients are better by our treatments here, they will not come back.  Sometimes, also, a patient that has not gotten better with a treatment may switch to another doctor and not give you another chance to make them better.  Stress that you want the patient to return if they are not feeling better, and they just might.

 

Transport of Care

 

In cases where you think that a patient needs a higher level of care or admission to a hospital, you may meet some resistance on the part of the patient to go to the hospital.  The perception of the hospital here is different than what you may be used to:  I would go to the hospital for more intense treatment, a higher level of testing, and better around the clock care.  For the people here, they go to the hospital to die, and thus only in the most grave of cases are they agreeable to go.  This has been frustrating to me at times, when I have felt limited in what I have been able to do with the resources that are available in the Clinic.  I do not recommend practicing outside of your abilities, but at the same time, remember that the patients have more TRUST in you than they do in the hospitals.  Oftentimes, they may not be admitted to the hospital without paying a fee up front, or bringing along the medicines that they need for their treatment.  Thus, in some ways, what you can provide is better than what a doctor in a hospital can do.  Also, you may send a patient to the hospital, where you already have the correct treatment in mind for the patient to receive.  The patient may go, and receive another treatment entirely.  For example, I had a patient with a closed head injury after a fall from a stairwell.  Immediately after the incident, she had cervical neck pain, aniscocoria, headache, and nausea.  I sent her to the hospital for neck Xrays, and a CT scan if possible.  They instead did skull Xrays to rule out a skull fracture, and took her out of the neck brace in which I had placed her without neck Xrays.  So treatments that may seem obvious to you in your own country are not necessarily practiced here.  Another example:  a patient who was sent with a fixed immobile neck mass for a cancer evaluation, who received an ultrasound and antibiotics, without a biopsy nor pathology evaluation.  FOR THAT REASON, I NOW SEND PATIENTS WITH A REFERRAL LETTER DESCRIBING MY DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS, WHAT TESTS I WOULD LIKE, AND WHAT TREATMENT I INITIATED, IF ANY.

 

The closest hospital is in Zamora, an hour away by bus.  Remember that the patient is then subject to the bus schedule, and a bumpy unpaved road to the hospital.  There are hospitals in Loja, 2-3 hours away by bus.  In this region, the people seem to consider the medical care in Cuenca to be superior, a city that is 8 hours away by bus, and often the poorest patients cannot afford this.

 

Some examples of this attitude towards hospitals over the course of my time here:

 

(1)    My very first day of work, a Saraguro man came with blackened necrotic fingertips on the 3rd and 4th digits on his Right hand.  He had suffered a partial amputation, and another doctor had sutured the ends shut, causing swollen deadened fingers.   I wanted him to the go to the hospital to have debridement of the necrotic fingertips, but when he refused to go, I did a digital nerve block and did it myself.

 

(2)    The care of pregnant women is free in the hospital.  Thus, I have encouraged women to have their deliveries in the hospital, as if something were to go wrong, we are limited in our abilities here.   One night at 10 PM, we were called to a woman’s house.  She was in labor, and had refused to go to the hospital beforehand for her delivery.  By the time that we reached her house, it would have been too risky to transport her.  So, I, with the help of the nurse Amanda, attended a delivery, in a shack, without electricity, with two candles to guide us.

 

(3)    I had a 17 year old patient come in a week after cutting his leg on his machete.  His mother had gotten a prescription for Naproxen and Penicillin, but only bought the Naproxen.  The boy came in with a red, swollen, hot leg, draining pus.  The mother was more concerned regarding putting natural oils on his leg than understanding the medicines that I had prescribed, and refused to take him to the hospital, saying that there was not enough money.  This put me in a tough spot:  If I continued to give IV injections or oral medications, I was letting her get away with not going to the hospital, giving her the message that I would keep giving him medicine, even though he may have eventually worsened.  Eventually, after 24 hours of talking to her and trying to tell her that he could die from this infection, the uncle intervened to take him, and he remained hospitalized for 2 months getting debridements of his leg.

 

Common Social Problems

 

Not unique to this area of the world, a variety of social problems exist, which may or may not be more prevalent here that what you may be used to.  In addition, the cultural differences may add another layer of difficulty in treating these problems.

 

(1)    Alcoholism:  Recently, there has been a town meeting in Guadalupe to address this exact problem.   The people here drink a great deal, but I have found them to be rarely honest with the quantity of alcohol they are drinking.  They answer “De repente, de repente” (meaning every once in awhile), but then I see them passed out on the Hanging Bridge several days later.  A LOT OF BINGE DRINKING OCCURS.  Family members occasionally are enablers of this drinking, either participating with the alcoholic, or not being honest that a real problem exists.  I have seen cases of alcoholic withdrawal, but they are difficult to treat, as the patients often do not follow-up. 

 

(2)    Domestic Violence:  The cases of domestic violence that I have heard over the course of the year are some of the saddest aspects of working in Guadalupe.  The low status of women, their lack of education, their inability to find work outside the home to provide for themselves and a large brood of children, all contribute to women often being powerless to defend themselves against the violence of men, or to leave when they are victims of violence.  Because of the culture of machismo, in addition to the teachings of the Church, women are not encouraged to leave an abusive situation.  They are also hesitant to speak up that abuse occurs.  Being in a situation where little resources exist to help the women escape makes helping them difficult, so I at least try to ask the questions regarding abuse so that the women are aware that I care about the issue, even if I have little to offer to better the situation.  We are hoping to offer self defense classes in the future, and have been trying to do education with men regarding alcoholism and abuse.

 

(3)    Sexual Abuse and Rape:  Over the course of my year, I have had several women confess to me that they had been raped as teenagers or abused as children.  Invariably, the women are ashamed and have not confessed to family members that the abuse has occurred.  In most cases, the victim is blamed for the abuse, thus making them afraid to speak out that the abuse even occurred.  I have had the opportunity to speak to some parents regarding their children who were abused, and help them understand that putting the blame on their children is the incorrect method.  In addition, in this conservatively Catholic country, many people can barely even name the private parts, nor talk openly about sex, which makes bringing this topic out into the open more difficult.

 

(4)    Machismo:   It exists.  I may be an educated foreign doctor, but I still get catcalls in the halls of the Clinic from men who think this is an appropriate way to get my attention.  In the town of Guadalupe, as well, men will whistle or hiss.  It is also surprising to me how little the women expect from the men here.  For example, if you ask if they are in a monogamous relationship, the women cannot tell you for sure.  There is always some level of mistrust of the fidelity of the men.  The men who leave, for example, to work in Spain, for a time, tell me that they cannot be expected to go without female companionship, thus breaking their wedding vows. 

 

(5)    Teen Pregnancy:  People initiate sexual activity when they are still teenagers, and it has not been unusual to see 16 year olds who are having their second babies.  Especially for the young girls, who have little chance of education if they do not learn birth control or abstinence, they become unmarried single mothers, often with the fathers of their children providing little or no support in the care of the children they make together.  There is little understanding of birth control, and little desire by the men to use it.  Boys of 16 or 17 years old are already paying for the services of prostitutes, and not using condoms.

 

(6)    Sexually Transmitted Diseases:  I have seen a variety of STDS in the Clinic, from genital warts to chancroid to Trichomonas.  I believe that we probably under-diagnose PID.  The patients are uneducated about STDS, and when asked if they had one in the past, often cannot give you a clearcut answer.  In a recent conference on HIV in the region, it was noted that there are 15 cases of HIV in the Zamora-Chinchipe region.  I have sent many patients to have the test done, but so far have not found any positives.  As mentioned above, with the large number of boys who confess using the services of prostitutes without the protection of condoms, I am very fearful that HIV could explode in this region.  From what people have told me, prostitution is legal here and the legal prostitutes are card-carrying, being tested every several months for STDs.  However, in the countryside where we are located, I doubt that all prostitution is occurring legally. 

 

(7)    Birth Control:  As a Mission Clinic, we do not have birth control available to give the people.  Oftentimes, patients do want to discuss birth control, and I have felt free to do that, using medical statistics regarding which methods are most reliable.  However, if the patient wants a form of birth control, we do not have it in the Clinic.  In Zamora, one hour away, a clinic named Aprofe receives patients who are interested in birth control, and offers a variety of methods. 

 

Common Chief Complaints

 

To give you a better idea of some of the medical complaints that you will be facing, here is a list of common complaints, and perceptions of illness.

 

(1)    Probably the number one thing that I see is musculoskeletal pain, the most common being low back pain.  The patient may come in with “dolor de los riñones” or kidney pain.  They think that this pain comes from their kidneys, and that kidney pain also manifests itself as burning with urination, and burning of the feet.  When I have seen how hard the people work, carrying logs on their backs, macheting for the whole day in the hot sun, walking for hours to reach their farms, miners bent over in a tunnel all day, it is not surprising how much pain they have.  You may treat their pain now, but chances are, with their way of working, the pain will return.  I usually try to include some stretching or exercises as part of treatment.  In addition, I am probably more liberal than most with the muscle relaxants, but I really think a lot of the people have back spasm.  I also try to be really clear that the pain does NOT come from their kidneys, but their muscles that are tense from their heavy labor.  When you take the time to explain, it helps them in the future not accept “kidney treatments” from other doctors.  I also try to explain very clearly that the pain does not come from a vitamin deficiency, thus receiving vitamin shots does nothing to help the problem.  Musculoskeletal pain is probably the most common reason that other doctors prescribe Vitamin B shots, so it pays to take the time to do some health education in this area.  In addition, I always try to include some sort of exercise or stretching regimen to avoid the pain coming back in the future.

 

(2)    Dysuria:  I have seen lots of patients with chronic complaints of dysuria, but the urine screening exam is usually negative for infection.  I think that the patients have such concentrated urine for lack of hydrating themselves while working in the hot sun, that their urine burns. 

 

(3)    Bitter taste in mouth (“boca amarga”), bruxism, bad breath (“mal aliento”):  These complaints are all thought to be due to parasites or liver problems.  Most of the time, bad breath can be attributed to poor dental hygiene, lack of brushing the teeth, and lack of taking out their dentures AT ALL for cleaning.   If a parasite treatment has not been given in 6 months, I will also treat for parasites.  Also consider Giardia and reflux in the differential diagnosis.

 

(4)    Dizziness (“mareos”):  The patients think that this is due to elevated cholesterol, but often may have evidence of high blood pressure, arrhythmia, or orthostasis.

 

(5)    “Nervios”:  The patients often think that they suffer from “nervios,” meaning that they are people who are very easily frightened, sensitive, anxious.  Parents often call their children “muy nervioso” for many reasons:  if the child cannot study well, if they scare easily, if the mother was in bad mood when she was pregnant with that child.  It seems to be a self-fulfilling prophecy more than anything else.  These patients tend to be very dramatic, and even worse, completely certain that something is wrong with them.  With the parents, I try to talk about not putting these “nervous” ideas into the child’s heads, as I have seen children faking attacks and fainting spells, from “nervios,” when in reality, they were having problems with a brother leaving for work in Spain.  Other times, I have found people with real depression or anxiety problems that needed medical treatment.  I try to explain that “nervios” as an illness in its own right does not exist, and there is usually a problem in the home, within a marriage, other stress that causes physical symptoms.  I joke with them that the custom here is to say “nervios” but a real illness with that name does not exist, and that they have to examine their lives to find out from where the stress comes.  The people understand when you explain it to them in this manner.

 

(6)    Fever:  Patients often call the site of their pain a source of “fiebre.”  They may not have had a real fever, and they have no way to measure their temperature at home.  For example, a patient with gastritis may say that he has a fever in his belly, or a woman with a headache may say that she has a fever in her head.  It takes a little further questioning to figure out if this was a real fever or not.  Complicating matters is the fact that the nurses do not get temperatures on all patients, only those that report fever, thus probably missing some patients with real fever.

 

(7)    “Aire”:  Patients use the term “aire” as a possible reason that they have various complaints.  They do not use this to describe problems with breathing, shortness of breath, or chest tightness.  They say that they have headache and the reason is “falta de aire.” 

 

(8)    VITAMINS:  Usually at the end of the visit, a patient will slip in that they need vitamins.  Some patients are up front, blaming their chief complaints on a “lack of vitamins.”  Like I have mentioned numerous times before, I tend to NOT give vitamins to feed into this desire for vitamins, when probably they are not necessary, when education on the balanced diet is sufficient.  I tell the patients that the custom here is to focus a great deal of attention on vitamins, when at the same time, they need to remember that other things are essential:  calories to give energy and force to the body, and proteins, fats, carbohydrates which play their own role in maintaining health.  I tell them that to focus only on vitamins is not correct, and only the DIET can give them everything that they need.  This is another instance that spending several minutes on health education can pay off.  It would be easy to write the patient off by giving them vitamins, but then we are forgetting our responsibility to educate.

 

(9)    Lack of appetite:  This is probably the most common reason that parents bring their children in.  When you do further questioning, you realize that yes, the children do have appetite, but are satisfying their hunger with candy, cola, sweets, and then have no room to eat food.  With these parents, I talk about restricting sweets, review the balanced diet, and give a parasite medication.  I review the child’s growth chart with the parents to show them that the child is growing normally, thus is receiving enough nutrition to maintain a normal weight.  I DO NOT GIVE VITAMINS unless there is a clearcut growth deficiency.  And I ask the parents to follow up every 6 months for a measurement of the growth, and to repeat the parasite medication.

 

 

Special Pediatric Considerations

 

  • Do not use oral quinolones in children less than 18 years of age.  Studies in beagle puppies (!!) have shown damage to cartilage.  At this time, we do not use quinolones in pediatric patients except in extreme emergency.

     

  • Do rectal temperatures in patients less than 6 months of age, for the most reliable measurements.  THE NURSES SHOULD KNOW THIS.  We use greater than 100.4 degrees as the cutoff for fever.  Remember that a fever can significantly raise the pulse and respiratory rate in a child.  Have the nurses treat the fever with Motrin 10 mg/kg or Tylenol 15 mg/kg so that by the time you see the child, you can see if the elevated respiratory rate was due to the fever or underlying respiratory disease.

     

  • Growth Problems in Children

 

If a child is small on the growth chart, there are a variety of things to consider.  There is nutritional and organic failure to thrive, and it is very RARE to see a case of organic failure to thrive. 

 

The first step in evaluating a small child is to do a midparental height.  To do a midparental height, add 5 cm to the height of the mother in a boy patient, OR subtract 5 cm from the height of the father in a girl patient to account for sex difference.  Average the corrected heights of both parents.  This is the midparental height.  Mark this on the growth chart.  In general, this is the height that the child can be expected to fulfill, accounting for the heredity from both parents.  Most children are growing in accordance with this midparental height, making them small for the hereditary of their parents.

 

If you are worried about the initial short stature or low weight of a child, look to see if they are symmetric.  A child low on weight and normal on height (or disproportion height and weight) LACKS CALORIES.  The parents may want vitamins, but are better off encouraging high calorie foods, such as milk, cheese, butter, yogurt, eggs, peanut butter, avocadoes, and beans.  I do not start vitamins right away in these cases, but discuss a change in the diet.  (The parents can add extra cheese and margarine to all that the kid eats.)  I am more likely to start a nutritional supplement (powder added to milk or water to give vitamins AND CALORIES) rather than just vitamins, again, trying to avoid this thought that vitamins cure all.  I try to STRESS CALORIES. 

 

If the child is proportional, they may be short and thin for familial reasons, and I recommend to follow up with them in 2 to 3 months to remeasure a height and weight, and follow their growth velocity.

 

  • I have left a Pediatric Drug Dosing Handbook in addition to a Harriet Lane.  We dose medicine by weight. 

Some common illnesses with dosages: 


--Acute Otitis Media with or without perforation:  Amoxicillin 80 mg/kg divided BID for 10 days (first line), Augmentin 45 mg/kg divided BID for 10 days (second line), 2nd generation cephalosporin (3rd line).  With a perforation, I add a quinolone ear drop (or eye drop depending on what is available) with an ear wick.  In older children, 3 to 5 years old, it is fine to decrease the length of duration of treatment to 5 to 7 days, if that makes it easy with the medication supply and concentrations that we have.


--UTI:  Think of UTI in young girls, especially, or uncircumcised males less than 1 year of age, especially with vomiting, diarrhea and fever.  Bactrim would be a good drug choice, in addition to Amoxicillin or Cephalexin.


--Diarrhea:  There is a lot of chronic diarrhea.  With vomiting, I more likely think of acute gastroenteritis.  Without vomiting, chronic nonspecific diarrhea of childhood is more common, and caused by too many soups and juices and coladas, instead of fatty foods and milk.  Parasites are also something to consider.   Acute diarrhea with blood would point to infectious diarrhea, and a good choice would be bactrim.  Treatment of infectious diarrhea with antibiotics has a slight risk of HUS (Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome).


--Parasites:  In less than 2 year olds, we use Piperazine (Lombrizina), as the parasites can travel per the esophagus and asphyxiate a child.  Piperazine has a paralytic which renders the parasites unable to move.  In greater than 2 year old child, we use Albendazole 400 mg.

 

 

Emergencies

 

On occasion, emergencies have shown up and needed stabilization or initial treatment before transport to the hospital.  We are limited in our capabilities to do much in these situations, secondary to inadequate nursing care/support help, medicines, and equipment.

 

We do have a crash cart.  When I arrived, the crash cart had a resuscitation kit with smelling salts, and glucose gel, from 1992, and that was it!!!  In the course of this year, we have restocked the crash cart as best we could with our supplies.  The crash cart now has several vials of epinephrine, atropine, lidocaine, insulin, and glucose tablets.  We kept the smelling salts for good measure.  We also have IV starting equipment, including intraosseous needles and central line kits.  We have one thoracotomy tray.  For airway management, we have ET tubes of various sizes (none pediatric), oral airways, nasopharyngeal airways, a laryngoscope and bag mask capability.  WE DO NOT HAVE OXYGEN.

 

Not all the staff here are current on BLS nor ACLS.   

 

Living at a Mission

 

Living and working in Guadalupe is a unique experience all its own.  You are living at a Catholic Mission with an international flavor, and I am sure that this is one of the few missions that exists in the world with such an international crew.  In my time, we have had nationals from the US, Ecuador, Colombia, Austria, Germany, Italy, and Ireland.  We speak Spanish, English, and German.  People of a variety of religious faiths have lived and worked in the Mission.

 

First and foremost, you are living in a Catholic Mission, and will be eating your meals and spending time with Sisters of the Teresita order of nuns.  A great side benefit of working in Guadalupe is the chance to know the Sisters, talk with them, and spend time with them.  They have a wonderful sense of humor, and are very caring.   Padre Jorge, the priest who founded the Clinic and who has lived in Guadalupe for over 10 years, is a very approachable, fun, and understanding person, who is very appreciative of all the work that you do.  Part of the most enjoyable part of working here is becoming a part of this unique family.

 

Because you are living at a Catholic mission, prayers are said before and after meals, and Catholic Mass is often held at the Church near the Clinic.  You are welcome to attend Mass, but it is not a requirement.  Being a non-Catholic myself, I have enjoyed participating in the religious aspect of life here, because it is an important part of the culture of the patients, and my friends here. 

 

That being said, oftentimes, the rigidity of the schedule of the Convent can oftentimes be a bit oppressive.  The meals are at 7:30, 12:00 and 6 PM every day, and when the Clinic schedule is full, you may not leave until 6 PM.  I have occasionally enjoyed taking a day to myself, to cook for myself in the Residence, or take some time to go for a walk or play basketball before dinner.  Thus, guard your free time, if you like to exercise, cook, or just want a day off to eat on your own, feel free to do that. 

 

The Volunteers’ Residence

 

You will live with the other volunteers at the Mission in a less than 2 year old residence behind the Clinic.  There are single and double rooms, each with its own bathroom.  There is a kitchen in the Residence, equipped with the basics in order to cook, including refrigerator, stove, oven and microwave.  We do have a TV with a VCR, in addition to a computer, with internet access.  We recently got a generous gift of a DVD player from two former volunteers (and good friends of mine).  In addition, we are lucky to have a washing machine for the clothing, which we then hang to dry on a clothesline.

 

Compared to how the people live in the town of Guadalupe, we are living in comparative luxury!  Every once in awhile, just to remind us of how lucky we are, the power will go out, or the water will go low.  Then we appreciate so much more all that we do have in the Residence!

 

The Climate

 

The weather here can change in the blink of an eye:  it is unpredictable! 

 

This region of Ecuador is particularly humid during the day, and can be cold at night.  Be prepared for cold weather, with layers of fleece, long sleeve shirts, and pants.   For the women, I have been able to wear shorts in town to play basketball and go on hikes without problems.  Layers are probably sufficient; it is not so cold that you need a winter jacket.  Definitely bring gear for the rain!  We have a supply of rubber boots that reach the knee that you can borrow during your time here to hike. 

 

Because of the humidity, we are constantly battling mold.  Mold forms very easily and clothes, your suitcase, anything that is left out in your room.  It is not uncommon to find mold forming on clothes left outside to dry, shoes left in your room…. Sometimes it feels like a losing battle!!

 

My Vision for the Clinic

 

Working here for a year has been challenging, but rewarding.  I have learned a great deal about myself, raised my confidence level as a doctor and pediatrician practicing on her own, and come to understand a great deal about the culture and practices here.  From my year here, and being the first longstanding doctor volunteer, I have a special personal investment in the Clinic and in the manner in which I would like to see it progress.  Padre Jorge has set a vision that we will provide a standard of care above and beyond what is here in Zamora Chinchipe, and I second this high ideal. 

 

I have seen the Clinic grow a great deal in my year here.  We saw the first eye and ENT surgeries performed in our operating rooms.  We expanded our patient population by thousands as the word of our quality care continues to grow.  We have had a growth in the number of volunteers.  We have renovated the nursing station with paintings on the wall for children, added bookcases to various rooms, CLEANED OUT A LOT OF JUNK.  In addition, we have increased in our efforts to do basic health education as part of the medical visits, with emphasis on balanced diet rather than vitamins, wound care, exercise, care of the body while working. 

 

However, in some ways, we are still lacking, and could do better.  Obviously, we are working in a different environment than what we may be used to in our own homes, as our patients travel for hours to receive our care, may not be able to return expeditiously to follow up on treatment, and the cultural barrier may at times be difficult.  In the future, we hope to expand the lab and have higher level of nursing care to help Amanda. 

 

First of all, I think we should have a level of pride regarding the quality care that we provide.  The following are things that I have been fighting for in my time here, and with my leaving, I hope that I can leave this mission to you, those who follow me, in helping this Clinic to be all that it can be.  That being said, I would like to see us really strive to raise it another notch in three ways:

 

(1)    For everything that we can do, do it to the best of our ability.  For example, doing complete vital signs on every patient, a simple intervention, yet so pertinent to quality care, needs to be continued.  When we withhold doing the things that can be done easily for lack of time or patience, such as vital signs, we are not providing quality care.  When we do lab tests, do them correctly:  clean catch midstream urines were not being done until I instructed the nurses how to do them.  Obese people were having blood pressures done with a normal adult cuff.  This is BASIC MEDICAL CARE, and when we fail to do this correctly, we are failing in our mission.

 

(2)    The Clinic should strive to have the best and CORRECT care for the patients.   Oftentimes, I see us practicing a bit of hypocrisy, and slowly slowly slowly things are changing.  One example:  we tell the patients to brush their teeth and not eat sweets, yet we only recently decided to stop selling candy in the pharmacy after much debate and discussion.  (The dentists who have recently filled cavities LOVE this.)  Thankfully the change occurred, but it has been a long time in coming, and we now have sugar free cookies and candies at the pharmacy.  Another example:  I preach against vitamin shots, yet we have them in the pharmacy.   Right now, other doctors are using the pharmacy and prescribing things like Vitamin B shots, and for that reason, we continue to buy Vitamin B shots, but my hope is that in the future, the people will be educated enough to NOT WANT these things.  It would be my hope that the Clinic will stop carrying worthless medicines that waste the patients´ money, and are bought from anyone other than a certified drug representative.  We cannot save nor attend to everyone in the region, but we can set an example of the medicine that we will and will not carry, setting a standard for what medicines work and what does not.   The WHO and Doctors without Borders both have guidelines for pharmacies that can be used to help us in making decisions on what medicines to buy, although many of the medicines that we receive in donations are much more sophisticated that the basics that they have listed.

 

(3)    Health Education is very important, and basic health concepts like vitamins, balanced diet, wound care, growth, pregnancy, women’s issues, alcoholism, all are very important and pertinent to what we do.  I hope that this may be continued, with more outreach into the barrios to educate the people regarding these basic health fundamentals.   It is important to stress these things in treatment discussions with the patients, as I try to tell them that these things are just as important, and less expensive, than buying a lot of medicine.

 

(4)    Right now, when we have specialists arriving, the Clinic is understaffed to deal with the large crowds of people waiting, and the Clinic staff is stretched even thinner when the volunteer doctors do not speak Spanish.  It would be nice to see additional nursing help and two doctors at all times to attend sufficiently to the people.  It would be nice to have one qualified and up to date nurse who can help with the growing laboratory  and patient support needs, and two doctors who can attend patients and collaborate with each other.  Amanda is currently serving as nurse, pharmacist, translator, lab tech, and all around support person, and is overworked.

 

I have put a lot of time and thought, to the best of my ability, into these patients, but I am sure that I made mistakes over the course of my time here.  I am a pediatrician who has been seeing kids of all ages, and when I started, a little out of practice with hypertension, type II diabetes, menopause.  I hope that my notes have been complete and helpful, and my suggestions useful.
