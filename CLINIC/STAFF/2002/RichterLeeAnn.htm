My experience in Guadalupe

I spent a wonderful 3 ½ months in Guadalupe working mostly in outreach and education but also in the clinic as a medical translator, taking medical histories, and doing vital signs.  Although I am not a physician, I worked very closely with many who came through and know what it is like to work with the patients and generally which approaches work well.  After my time in Guadalupe I have a pretty good idea of what life is like in the clinic, at the mission, and as a member of the town.  Based on my pre-trip concerns and what I learned, these suggestions will help anyone who is considering volunteering.

 Most importantly…

            Learn Spanish, as much as you can- for your sake and for the people.  It is easier to pick up Spanish if you come having at least some background.  Life is much more fun if you can communicate with the people you work with, the old women who sit all day on their porches, the shopkeepers selling fried pork, and the kids that run around town with popsicles.

It is not an intimidating environment to practice and use Spanish.  The sisters and community are very supportive and appreciate when foreigners try to speak Spanish but it is always best to have as much background as you can.  From experience, I truly believe it is more fulfilling as well.

 

About packing… Less is More!

  1. Bring only quick drying clothes as they may have trouble drying regardless!  Your clothes will get dirty and because of the humidity could get moldy so bring older things that you may not mind getting rid of.  You will not need or want hiking boots- old sneakers/walking shoes are better and should there be rain or mud you will want to be wearing the rubber boots the clinic provides anyhow.
  2. Bring a light, cheap poncho. Its better coverage than a Gore-Tex-type coat, lighter, and easier to carry with you. (When it rains, it rains hard and comes out of no where.)
  3. The clinic has a great supply of movies and a growing book collection; don’t weigh down your bags with a library of books!
  4. Sunglasses and a hat with a good brim are a must, I never used bug spray in over 3 months, long pants are much better.
  5. It’s nice to have a water bottle that you can take with you on walks and carry around with you during the day.
  6. Bring small bills and change; it is difficult for people to make change from 20s and sometimes even 10s.  There is VERY little to buy in Guadalupe and most of everything that you will need is at the clinic.

 About mission life:

  1. It is respectful if you are at meals on time and if you feel comfortable reciting the before and after meal prayers (whether or not you are Catholic), it seems very much appreciated if you learn them.
  2. Regardless of your religious background, Mass is a cultural as well as religious celebration and I found it to be a great way to get incorporated into the community and get a feel for life in Guadalupe.
  3. If given the opportunity to accompany Padre Jorge Nigsch to other villages, GO! Surrounding Guadalupe are amazing villages, each with subtle cultural differences and it is incredible to see how these communities live and to take part in their cultural celebrations.

 About working at the clinic:

  1. There are many sets of nice scrubs for the volunteers; you will not need to pack them.
  2. Having seen many doctors come through, there is no standard way to work at the clinic, each person runs their office a bit differently reflecting their talents.  Getting a good feel for the culture through observation, sensitivity, and talking with Padre and Amanda seems to make the most effective patient interactions.

 About your patients:

            There are 3 groups that generally come in, the Shuar indigenous group, the Saraguro indigenous group, and Mestizos.  Many of the indigenous patients may have never visited a doctor before and therefore do not know the normal procedure.  Therefore, they may spend a long time trying to explain what is wrong, exaggerate their complaint, or come out with the most major complaint just as you think you have addressed all their concerns.  It is helpful to keep this in mind so that you can alter your examination routine accordingly (some doctors choose to ask specifically at the beginning of the appointment the most major problem or to list the most important 3 complaints.) 

 You will find that most patients want to be treated with an injection that, like magic, will cure their symptoms and may seem unsatisfied if you do not.  Likewise, a patient may seem unsatisfied if you suggest a lifestyle change, such as stopping smoking or exercise as opposed to prescribing a “miracle drug” which will solve the problem for them.  It is important therefore to make the patient seem empowered by their ability to improve their own health and to spell out the positive side for them, that they do not need to spend money on medicine but rather are in control of the situation themselves.

 Patients also may have trouble understanding the idea of a chronic illness that will be with them for their life.  It is important to be clear in your explanations (that a chronic illness can not be cured, but that things can be done to calm symptoms, slow progression, and improve quality of life with the disease.)  It may feel that you are spelling out too much for the patient but it is surprising how much people do not understand about taking medication, diseases, and health –and much better that they know how to take the medicine, know the name of their medicine and have a clear but basic idea of why they are taking their medicine. 

About learning more:

I would really love to talk with anyone who is interested in working at the clinic.  I know I had many questions and concerns before I made the trip, please do not hesitate to get in touch with me should you feel the same way.  Working in Guadalupe was an incredible experience for me as it is for so many of the volunteers- I would be happy to share the joy I found in this place with others!

Lee Ann Richter

The Unpaved Road to Recovery

Guadalupe, Ecuador 2004

During the 4 months I spent working and teaching in a rural health clinic I thought I had learned the most important concepts of clinical care. But it was a house call, 3 hours from the closest village, where I witnessed how to administer the most valuable and intangible treatment.

After 3 hours on a rickety crowded bus, we still had 3 more to go to reach our patient. The roads in this part of southern Ecuador did not allow for fast traveling. Beena, the American doctor, and I rode from our small village for 3 hours until the dirt road dead-ended in another village. It was the last village accessible by bus, where we then had to mount horses to ride into the Andes. The patient's father met us with horses to travel the 3 remaining hours to his home.

Until this time all we knew was that his son was 18 years old and had been unable to walk for 3 years. He had slowly developed a paralysis that crept up his body and weakened him until he was no longer able to get out of bed. Mentally he was alert, aware and intelligent as other 18 year olds, however his body had failed him, succumbing to the paralysis and gradually seizing as his muscles atrophied from lack of use. The father could not explain his son's illness, but seemed assured that this doctor from the United States would be able to give his son an injection that would reverse the effects of 3 years. His reasoning was that he knew others who had received injections and were suddenly cured of paralysis-like symptoms. He felt confident that this American doctor would make his son walk again.

Beena, the young physician whom I had been helping in the rural health clinic was nervous about this house call, as was I. Before we left the clinic, she had read up on polio, believing that to be a likely cause of this man's symptoms. Together we brainstormed ways of helping improve the quality of life for this bedridden man given the fact that his family had no source of income, there were no medical resources in the vicinity, and any medicine we could leave with his family would most likely not be able to be replaced once it ran out. How could we give the most to this young man without having many resources to rely on?

The wooden slat house was perched on a hillside with the Andean mountains in the background. Beena and I were introduced to the mother as the younger siblings ran around the yard, peaking around corners to stare and whisper. I was curious as to how Beena would perform this unusual consultation. We were brought in to see the patient in his room. He looked young, much younger than 18, and his body was seized up and skinny- clearly he would never walk again. I felt chills standing in the room that had made up this man's entire world for the last 3 years. His eyes, however, expressed a different message than the one his shriveled body projected. Vitality hidden within his atrophied frame escaped thru his eyes and radiated in the room.

I watched as Beena walked into the room and approached the patient, looking into his eyes calmly and genuinely expressing pleasure to meet him. She sat on a cot beside his bed and went through a detailed medical history, directing his story chronologically but otherwise letting him tell his tale. When she asked what he hoped she could do for him, his mother interjected that she expected Beena could make her son walk again, that she must have an injection that would cure her son. Our patient, however, did not make such statements; he seemed to be more understanding of his own situation.

What Beena then did was remarkable. She told the blatant and disheartening truth in the most respectful and humane way she could have. I watched her explain to the parents that what their son had could not be cured by an injection she had, nor could he be made able to walk again by anything that the medical world could offer. That being said, she went through all the things that were in great health with their son and what they could do, together with him, to improve his quality of life. She not only described exercises and massages they could help him with but showed them how to do them, making sure they understood.

What Beena gave this family was more than anything she could have provided in resources, whether in rural Ecuador or in the Unites States. She left them with an honest reality, but more importantly, she left them informed as to how to make that reality as rewarding as it could possibly be. She left an intangible but unending gift, knowledge empowering them to make beneficial changes in the quality of their son's life.

 Lee Ann Richter
